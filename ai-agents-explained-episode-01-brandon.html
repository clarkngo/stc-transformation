FACULTY: Alright. Before I explain anything, I just want to show you this.

[SCREEN]

Screen recording: a simple, clean chat interface. Like WhatsApp but professional. No code visible anywhere. The faculty member’s cursor moves naturally — this should feel like a real demo, not a polished explainer.

FACULTY (VO): This is an AI agent. It’s running right now. Watch what happens when I type to it.

[SCREEN] Types into the chat:

UserHey, can you help me prep for my 3pm meeting with the sales team?

[SCREEN] Agent responds — takes about 2 seconds:

AgentOf course. I pulled last month’s figures from your shared drive and noticed Q3 revenue was down 8% versus target. Your team’s two biggest open deals are with Nexus Corp and Patel Industries — both have follow-ups due this week. Want me to draft a quick summary you could share at the start of the meeting?

[PIP] Faculty face appears in corner — reacting genuinely

FACULTY: Okay — so. A few things just happened there that I want to unpack.

First — I didn’t tell it where my files were. I didn’t tell it to look at Q3 data. It knew to do that because of how it was set up.

Second — it didn’t just answer me. It’s offering to do something. Draft a summary. That’s the thing with agents.

They don’t just respond. They act.

[CAM]

FACULTY: And that one difference — respond versus act — is basically the whole video.

So let’s break it down properly.

CHAPTER 2 — “Okay but what actually IS an agent”

3:00 – 5:30

[CHAPTER] Chapter 2 — What actually is an agent

[CAM]

FACULTY: Here’s the simplest way I can put this.

You’ve probably used ChatGPT, or something like it. You type a question. It gives you an answer. Maybe you ask it to write something. It writes it. You ask it to make a list. It makes a list.

That’s a chatbot. Incredibly useful. But it’s — it’s like a really smart colleague who will answer any question you ask them. You ask, they answer, and then it’s on you to do something with that answer.

[SCREEN] Simple split-screen visual — no animation needed, just text on screen:

Chatbot / ChatGPT

You ask → It responds
You still have to DO the thing

AI Agent

You ask → It acts
It does the thing for you

[PIP]

FACULTY: An agent has the same brain — same AI underneath — but it’s been given tools. Connections to your calendar. Your email. Your files. Databases. Whatever it needs to actually go and do things, not just describe what doing things would look like.

Think of it this way.

[CAM]

A chatbot is like asking someone: “Hey, what would be a good response to this email?” And they tell you. And then you go write it.

An agent is like saying: “Handle this email.” And they handle it.

(beat — let it land)

Same intelligence. Very different outcome.

CHAPTER 3 — “The new employee thing — stay with me”

5:30 – 8:30

[CHAPTER] Chapter 3 — The new employee analogy

[CAM]

FACULTY: Okay so I want to use an analogy here that I think is actually really accurate — not just a nice metaphor, but genuinely how this works.

An AI agent is like a new employee.

Specifically — a new employee who is:

  • Incredibly fast
  • Always available
  • Never tired
  • And extremely, almost aggressively literal

[LOWER THIRD] New employee — fast, always on, extremely literal

FACULTY: That last one is the important one. Let me show you what I mean.

[SCREEN] Back to the chat demo — same clean interface

FACULTY (VO): Watch what happens when I give this agent a vague instruction versus a clear one.

[SCREEN] Types:

UserSummarize the report.

[SCREEN] Agent responds:

AgentI found 14 reports in your shared drive. Which one would you like me to summarize? Here are the most recent: Q3 Sales Report, IT Infrastructure Review, HR Onboarding Feedback (Oct), Marketing Campaign Analysis…

[PIP]

FACULTY: See that? I said “the report” like it would obviously know which one. It didn’t guess. It asked. Because it does exactly what it’s told — no more, no less.

[SCREEN] Types again:

UserSummarize the Q3 Sales Report — two paragraphs, for a non-financial audience.

[SCREEN] Agent responds with a clean two-paragraph summary

[CAM]

FACULTY: Now THAT worked. Because I told it exactly what I wanted.

(leaning in)

FACULTY: And here’s the big insight — the most important thing about any AI agent isn’t the AI part. It’s the instructions you give it before any of this starts.

In the industry they call this a system prompt. But honestly? It’s just a job description.

You write it before the agent starts working. It tells the agent: who you are, what you do, what you should and shouldn’t do. The agent reads that job description and then behaves accordingly — every single time, for every single person who talks to it.

[SCREEN] Show a simple job description on screen — plain text, no code:

Agent job description (example) You are an executive assistant supporting the senior leadership team. You have access to the shared drive, calendar, and email system. Your job is to help prepare for meetings, draft communications, and surface relevant data. You should always: — Confirm before sending any external email — Flag anything that looks unusual or urgent — Keep responses concise — busy people, short answers You should never: — Share salary or HR data — Access files outside the Leadership shared folder — Make financial commitments on behalf of anyone

[PIP]

FACULTY: That’s it. That’s the whole thing. No code. No engineering degree. That’s a job description, written by a human, that tells the agent how to behave in every situation.

And the quality of that job description? That’s what determines whether the agent is useful or a disaster.

[CAM]

FACULTY: Which means — and I want to be really clear about this — the most important skill for working with AI agents isn’t technical.

It’s clarity. It’s communication. It’s knowing what you actually want and being able to write it down precisely.

That’s a management skill. A leadership skill. A skill you already have.

[CHAPTER] Chapter 4 — What’s inside an agent (quick version)

[CAM]

Three parts. Super simple.

[PIP]

FACULTY (VO): The brain — this is the AI model at the center. It’s what understands language, reasons through situations, decides what to do. You don’t build this. You choose it. Think of it like hiring someone who went to a certain school — you’re choosing their background, not making it.

The job description — we just covered this. The instructions you write before the agent starts. The single most important thing you control.

And then memory — and this one surprises people.

[CAM]

FACULTY: Most AI agents, by default, have no memory between conversations.

Like — none.

Every new conversation, they start completely fresh. No idea who you are, no idea what you talked about last time. Every session is a blank slate.

(doing a mock surprised face)

I know. It’s weird.

It has real implications — which we’re going to dig into in episode three. But for now, just know: memory in AI agents is a design choice, not a given. Someone has to decide what the agent should remember and how long to keep it.

And again — that’s a judgment call, not a coding problem.

CHAPTER 5 — “Real examples — what does this actually look like at work”

10:30 – 12:30

[CHAPTER] Chapter 5 — What this looks like at work

[CAM]

FACULTY: Okay. Real world. Three quick examples. None of these require you to know anything technical.

[SCREEN] Title card: “Example 1: HR Support Agent”

Example 1: HR Support Agent

FACULTY (VO): A company with 500 new hires per quarter. Every single one has the same questions. Where’s the leave policy? How do I log expenses? When’s my first review?

Instead of five HR staff answering the same thing five hundred times — there’s an agent. Its job description says: you know the employee handbook, you answer HR questions, you escalate anything sensitive to a real person.

New employee chats with it. Gets an instant, accurate answer. HR team handles the things that actually need human judgment.

[SCREEN] Title card: “Example 2: Meeting Prep Agent”

Example 2: Meeting Prep Agent

FACULTY (VO): A senior leader has twelve meetings a week. Before each one, someone has to pull the relevant data, check who’s attending, note any open items.

That’s an hour of work, minimum, every single day. Just prep.

An agent with access to the calendar, the shared drive, and the email thread — and a clear job description — can produce a one-page brief before every meeting. Automatically. No one has to ask.

[SCREEN] Title card: “Example 3: First-pass Review Agent”

Example 3: First-pass Review Agent

FACULTY (VO): A legal or procurement team that receives fifty contracts a week. Before a senior person reviews any of them, someone has to do a first pass — flag the unusual clauses, check against the standard template.

An agent can do that first pass in minutes. Flag the things that need human eyes. The senior reviewer now spends their time on the flagged items — not on reading fifty clean contracts looking for the one weird clause.

[CAM]

FACULTY: Same pattern every time. Agent handles the high-volume, well-defined, repeatable work. Human handles the judgment-heavy stuff.

Not replacing anyone. Changing what they spend their time on.

An AI agent is software that acts on your behalf. Not just responds. Acts. It’s got a brain, a job description you write, and memory that resets unless you design it not to.

The quality of what it does is almost entirely determined by how clearly you defined the job. Which means the people who are going to get the most value out of AI agents in the next five years aren’t the people who can build them.

They’re the people who can direct them well.

And that’s exactly what this series is about.

The key adaptation from Brandon Hancock’s developer format

Brandon’s format is build-along — viewers watch code written and run in real time. That creates tension: will it work? The non-tech adaptation keeps that energy but shifts the source. Instead of will this code run? we get will the agent handle this request? The screen demos in Chapter 1 and Chapter 3 create the same live-discovery beat; the anchor reacts genuinely (“see that?” / “now THAT worked”). That authenticity is the signature of this pacing style.

Anchor delivery notes

  • Energy level. The most energetic of the three Episode 1 formats on this site. The anchor should feel genuinely excited to show something — not performed enthusiasm, but the natural energy of someone who uses the tools daily.
  • The “okay so” opener. Opens mid-conversation on purpose. Don’t swap for a formal cold open.
  • Commentary during the demo. PiP face: watching something familiar but still interesting; react; don’t narrate robotically.
  • Lean-in beats. When the script marks (leaning in), lean slightly toward camera and drop pace a hair — signals this is the line that matters.
  • “I read every single one.” Deliver simply and sincerely if you use that outro beat; it sets audience relationship at scale.

Screen demo notes for production team

  • Chat UI should feel like a real product — not a slide deck.
  • Agent responses: short natural delay (~1.5s). Not instant (reads fake), not slow (kills pace).
  • Demo type large enough for mobile viewers.
  • Fictional but realistic content; no real company names or personal data.
  • Job description on screen: Notion / Doc look — never a code editor.
