Script conventions

  • [STAGE] — physical blocking, prop use, movement
  • [BOARD] — whiteboard or slide: write this, leave it visible
  • [AUDIENCE] — moment involving the live audience / volunteers
  • [SLIDE] — clean, minimal slide (almost entirely black background, white text, single idea)
  • [PAUSE] — deliberate silence
  • [CARD] — instruction text on a physical card (shown to room / camera)
  • Words in bold — spoken with emphasis
  • (Italics) in direction lines — tone or direction note, not spoken
  • Malan-style verbal tics preserved: “So”, “right?”, “And so”, “Not to worry”

Pre-lecture setup

While audience is seated. Faculty visible on stage, adjusting things. A table is set up with items:

— A stack of printed envelopes, sealed
— A printed card face-down
— A marker and a large notepad on an easel (the “whiteboard”)
— Nothing else. Clean stage.

OPENING — “This. Is. AI Agents.”

Runtime: 0:00 – 1:30

[STAGE]

Faculty walks to center. Quiet. Makes eye contact with the audience. Waits a beat.

FACULTY: Let me ask you a question.

[PAUSE]

How many of you, in the last six months, have heard the words “AI agent” — in a meeting, in a news article, from someone in your IT department, somewhere — and nodded as though you understood what they meant?

[STAGE]

Looks around. Genuine, not confrontational.

FACULTY: And how many of you — if I’m being honest — weren’t entirely sure?

[PAUSE]

(warmly)

That’s most of the room. And that’s exactly right.

Because here’s what I’ve noticed. The people who build AI agents have done a remarkable job explaining them to other people who build AI agents. And a rather poor job explaining them to everyone else.

[STAGE]

Moves slightly forward. More direct.

FACULTY: So. Today we fix that.

By the end of this lecture, you will be able to answer — clearly and confidently — what an AI agent is, how it works, and why it matters to you specifically, regardless of whether you ever write a single line of code.

Not to worry if you haven’t. Most of the people in this room haven’t. That is precisely the point.

[STAGE]

Steps back. Arms out slightly — addressing the room.

FACULTY: This. Is. AI Agents.

[SLIDE] Black screen. White text, centered:

AI Agents, Explained
Lecture 0: What Is an AI Agent?

[STAGE]

Holds for one beat. Then moves to the board.

PART 1 — “Let’s start at the very beginning”

Runtime: 1:30 – 4:00

[BOARD]

Writes at the top, large and clear:

AGENT

FACULTY: So. The word itself.

Before we talk about AI agents, let’s talk about agents. Because the word is doing real work here, and I don’t want us to skip past it.

[BOARD]

Writes beneath:

Board(Latin: agere — to act, to do)

FACULTY: The word agent comes from the Latin agere — to act. To do something. An agent, in any context, is an entity that acts on someone else’s behalf.

You’ve heard this word before. You may not have thought about it this way.

[BOARD]

Writes a short list, pausing between each:

Board — Literary agent → acts on behalf of a writer
— Travel agent → acts on behalf of a traveler
— Real estate agent → acts on behalf of a buyer or seller
— Secret agent → acts on behalf of a government

(slight smile)

FACULTY: We’ll set aside the last one for now.

[PAUSE]

But notice what they all share. They don’t just advise. They act. They do something in the world on your behalf.

So when we say AI agent, we mean: an artificial intelligence that acts. Not one that simply responds to a question. Not one that generates text for you to review. One that does things.

[BOARD]

Underlines the word ACTS.

FACULTY: That one word — acts — is going to be the most important concept in this entire lecture. So let’s make sure we really understand what it means.

PART 2 — THE ENVELOPE DEMONSTRATION

“The thing you’ll remember from this lecture”

Runtime: 4:00 – 8:30

[STAGE]

Faculty picks up one of the sealed envelopes from the table. Holds it up.

FACULTY: I want to try something.

[AUDIENCE]

Addresses the room directly.

FACULTY: I need a volunteer. Someone who is willing to follow instructions very precisely. And when I say precisely, I mean: exactly as written. No interpretation. No filling in gaps. No assuming you know what I meant.

[STAGE]

Selects a volunteer. They come to the front.

FACULTY: Now. Inside this envelope is a set of instructions. Your job — your only job — is to do exactly what the instructions say. Nothing more. Nothing less.

[STAGE]

Hands the volunteer the envelope. They open it and read the card inside:

[CARD]

Instructions 1. Walk to the whiteboard.
2. Write the number 7 on it.
3. Return to your seat.

[STAGE]

The volunteer follows the instructions. Walks to the board. Writes 7. Returns.

FACULTY: Perfect. Thank you.

(to the audience)

What just happened?

[PAUSE]

We gave a person a set of written instructions. They had a brain — they could understand the instructions. They had a job description — those three steps. And they acted on it.

[BOARD]

BoardBrain + Job Description → Action

FACULTY: That is, in its simplest possible form, an AI agent.

Now — let me show you something more interesting.

[STAGE]

Picks up a second envelope. Calls up a second volunteer.

FACULTY: Same exercise. Follow the instructions exactly as written.

[STAGE]

Second volunteer opens envelope. Card reads:

[CARD]

Instructions1. Handle the emails.

[STAGE]

Volunteer looks at the card. Looks up at faculty. Looks back at card.

(gently)

FACULTY: Go ahead.

[STAGE]

Volunteer looks helplessly around the room.

FACULTY: Any questions?

[AUDIENCE]

Laughter.

FACULTY: Right. So. “Handle the emails” is — on its face — a complete instruction. It has a subject. It has a verb. But it raises immediately: which emails? Handle how? Reply? Delete? Forward? Prioritize? Archive?

The volunteer has a brain. They can understand the words. But the instruction is too vague to act on.

[BOARD]

BoardVague instructions → Confusion or wrong action

FACULTY: This is not a problem unique to artificial intelligence, by the way.

[PAUSE]

(drily)

This is a problem as old as management.

[AUDIENCE]

Some knowing laughter.

[STAGE]

Third envelope. Same volunteer, or a third one.

FACULTY: One more. Bear with me.

[CARD]

Instructions You are an assistant to the department head.
You have access to the shared inbox.
Your job is to read incoming emails and sort them into three categories:
— Urgent (needs a response today)
— Routine (can wait until end of week)
— FYI only (no response needed)

Do NOT reply to any email without being asked.
Do NOT delete anything.
If you are unsure which category an email belongs in, mark it “Uncertain” and flag it for review.

[STAGE]

Volunteer reads. Nods. Looks confident.

FACULTY: Now you could do something useful, yes?

[STAGE]

Volunteer nods.

(to the room)

Same brain. Three different job descriptions. Three completely different outcomes.

[BOARD]

Board Brain + “Handle the emails” → Confusion
Brain + Detailed job description → Useful, bounded action
Brain + No job description at all → Chaos

[PAUSE]

FACULTY: The brain of an AI agent is the AI model at its center. Off-the-shelf, powerful, general-purpose intelligence. You don’t build the brain — you choose it, the way you’d choose what kind of background someone comes from.

But the job description? That is entirely yours to write.

And as we just saw — the quality of what the agent does is almost entirely determined by the quality of that job description.

[STAGE]

Picks up the third card and holds it up.

FACULTY: In the industry, this is called a system prompt. Or sometimes just “instructions.” But I think the better word — the more honest word — is job description.

[BOARD]

BoardJob Description = System Prompt (they mean the same thing)

FACULTY: Not to worry. We’ll use both terms. But now you know what people mean when they say system prompt. They mean: the written instructions that tell the agent what to do, how to behave, and what not to do.

[PAUSE]

FACULTY: Any questions so far?

[STAGE]

Genuinely pauses. Scans the room.

PART 3 — “Responding versus acting”

Runtime: 8:30 – 11:30

[BOARD]

New section. Draws a line and writes:

Two kinds of AI

FACULTY: So. Now that we understand what an agent is, let me make a distinction that I think is genuinely important — and often glossed over.

There are, broadly speaking, two kinds of AI systems you’re likely to encounter.

[BOARD]

Writes, left side:

BoardConversational AI
(ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude…)

FACULTY: These systems respond. You give them input, they give you output. You ask a question, they answer. You ask them to write something, they write it. You ask them to analyze something, they analyze it.

(pausing)

And then — and this is the key part — you do something with the output. You copy it. You edit it. You send it. You use it. But you are the one who acts.

[BOARD]

Writes, right side:

BoardAI Agents
(can take actions in the world)

FACULTY: These systems act. They don’t just hand you a result — they use that result to do something. Send the email. Update the database. Book the meeting. File the report.

The intelligence underneath might be identical. The brain might even be the same AI model. But the agent has been given tools — connections to real systems in the world — so that it can act, not just respond.

[BOARD]

Draws an arrow between the two:

Board Conversational AI ——————→ AI Agent
                    + tools
                    + job description
                    + permission to act

FACULTY: So the question to ask about any AI system you encounter is simply: does it respond, or does it act?

If it responds — it’s a conversational AI. Extraordinarily useful. Not an agent.

If it acts — it’s an agent. Different capabilities, different risks, different governance questions.

[PAUSE]

FACULTY: More on that last point — the governance questions — later in this series. For now, let’s just hold the distinction.

[BOARD]

Boxes the arrow diagram. Leaves it visible.

PART 4 — “Three parts of every agent”

Runtime: 11:30 – 14:30

[BOARD]

Every agent has three parts.

FACULTY: Let’s build up a complete picture.

I want to give you a mental model that you can carry out of this room and apply to any AI agent you encounter — whether you’re evaluating a vendor, managing a team that’s deploying one, or just trying to understand what your organization is getting into.

Three parts. I’ll write them as we go.

[BOARD]

Writes number 1, then pauses for effect before writing the label:

Board1. The Brain

FACULTY: The brain is the AI model at the center. It’s what understands language, reasons about situations, and decides what to do next. In the industry, these are called large language models — LLMs. But for our purposes: the brain is the intelligence.

Importantly — you almost never build the brain yourself. You choose one. Think of it less like building an engine and more like hiring someone: you’re choosing what kind of background, training, and capability they bring.

[BOARD]

Board2. The Job Description

FACULTY: We’ve spent a lot of time here already. But let me add one layer.

A good job description for an AI agent has four things.

[BOARD]

Indented under Job Description:

Board — What the agent IS (role, name, context)
— What the agent DOES (tasks, scope)
— What the agent SHOULD NOT DO (limits, escalation)
— How the agent should BEHAVE (tone, format, precision)

FACULTY: Notice what is not on that list. Nothing technical. Nothing about programming. Nothing about models or parameters or architecture.

Writing a job description for an AI agent is a human task. A communication task. A management task. It requires clarity, judgment, and an understanding of what you actually want — all things that professionals in this room do every single day.

[BOARD]

Board3. Memory

FACULTY: This one surprises people. And it should.

By default — and I want to be precise about this: by default — most AI agents have no memory between conversations.

[PAUSE]

None.

Every new conversation begins completely fresh. The agent has no record of anything that was said before. No context. No history. A blank slate.

[STAGE]

Picks up a marker. Writes on a fresh part of the board:

[BOARD]

BoardDefault: Memory resets every conversation.

FACULTY: Now — you can give agents memory. There are ways to build it in. But it is a deliberate design choice, not a default feature.

And this matters because it has real implications for privacy, for consistency, and for how agents behave across long-running tasks. We’ll spend an entire lecture on memory later in this series. For now: just know that memory is a variable, not a constant.

[BOARD]

Steps back. The board now shows three numbered items with their labels.

FACULTY: So. Three parts.

Brain. Job description. Memory.

[PAUSE]

FACULTY: Any questions?

[STAGE]

Waits.

PART 5 — “So what do you do with this?”

The Problem Set setup

Runtime: 14:30 – 16:30

FACULTY: Let me close with something practical.

In CS50 — the course that inspired this series — every lecture ends with a problem set. A concrete challenge that takes what you’ve just learned and asks you to apply it.

So here is your problem set for Lecture 0.

[STAGE]

Picks up a fresh card — like the ones from the envelopes — and holds it up.

FACULTY: Think of one task in your current work — just one — that is: high in volume, repetitive, and well-defined.

Not a task that requires deep judgment. Not a relationship-critical task. Something that is done over and over, in roughly the same way, by a capable person who could be doing something more interesting instead.

And then write a job description for an AI agent that could do it.

[BOARD]

BoardYour job description must include:
1. What the agent IS
2. What the agent DOES
3. What the agent MUST NOT DO
4. How the agent should behave

FACULTY: Don’t worry about the technology. Don’t worry about whether this is technically possible. Don’t worry about implementation.

Just write the job description.

[PAUSE]

Because here is what I want you to discover for yourself — and I want you to discover it through doing, not through me telling you:

The act of writing a job description for an AI agent forces you to be precise about something you have probably never had to be precise about before: exactly what a task consists of, where it starts, where it ends, and what “done correctly” actually looks like.

[PAUSE]

(genuinely, not dramatically)

That precision is the skill.

Not the technology. Not the prompting. Not knowing which model to use.

The skill is knowing clearly what you want — clearly enough to write it down in a way someone else can follow exactly.

[STAGE]

Sets down the card. Steps to the center of the stage.

FACULTY: Next lecture, we’re going to look at what happens when job descriptions go wrong — and what makes one genuinely good. We’ll look at real examples side by side. And I think you’ll find it illuminating.

For now — take the card. Write the job description. Bring it back.

[STAGE]

Reaches into a small box on the table. Pulls out blank index cards — the same style as the ones from the envelopes. Holds them up.

(smiling)

Cards are on the table on your way out.

[PAUSE]

See you next time.

The envelope demonstration — detailed staging notes

This is the physical centerpiece of the lecture. It is the phone-book-tear equivalent. It must be rehearsed.

  • Envelope 1 (clear instructions): The volunteer succeeds. Establishes the baseline: agent = brain + instructions → correct action.
  • Envelope 2 (vague instructions): “Handle the emails.” The volunteer visibly stalls. The faculty doesn’t mock the volunteer — gently ask “any questions?” Make clear this is not the volunteer’s failure. “You have a brain. The instruction is the problem.”
  • Envelope 3 (good job description): The volunteer reads it, nods, looks confident. They don’t need to physically do anything — body language from envelope 2 to envelope 3 tells the story.
  • Pacing: Don’t rush between envelopes. Let each one land before moving on.

The problem set — why it matters

Problem sets work because they require producing something — not just understanding. The index card job description is this version’s deliverable.

  • Pedagogical — applies the four-part framework immediately, anchoring it in memory.
  • Strategic — with permission, real submissions can open Lecture 1 (“Someone in last week’s audience wrote this…”).

A note on the course numbering

CS50 counts from zero. Lecture 0. Week 0. This series uses the same device: zero marks the foundation; everything builds on it. The numbering signals a real course architecture, not a loose playlist.
