Deep Dives

What each format is really doing under the hood

Behind every stylistic choice is a theory of how people learn and what makes them act. These breakdowns make the implicit logic explicit.

Vox / Kurzgesagt

Concept as spectacle

"Make the invisible visible, then make it satisfying to understand."

What it's doing
Uses animation to externalise abstract ideas — the viewer never has to hold a complex mental model in working memory because it's rendered on screen. Each cut is a cognitive handoff.
Core mechanism
Dual coding (paired visual + narration) reduces load. Fast rhythm keeps attention without demanding active effort. The reveal structure (show problem → show consequence → show reframe) creates repeated micro-satisfactions.
Viewer walks away with
A sharable analogy. They can say "it's like…" to someone else. High share-intent, low recall of specifics after 48 hours without reinforcement.
Risk
Passive consumption can feel like learning without being learning. The viewer may overestimate their understanding ("fluency illusion").
STC use-case
Top-of-funnel awareness. Pre-enrollment. Social media clips. "What is AI?" for a non-technical audience that will never run code.
TED

Idea as personal stake

"Give the audience a belief they didn't have when they sat down."

What it's doing
Opens with a personal story that creates identity-level stakes, then widens to a universal truth. The speaker models the intellectual journey so the audience can mirror it emotionally rather than analytically.
Core mechanism
Narrative transportation — when someone is absorbed in a story, counter-arguing drops. The job-description question ("what does it know, what can it do, what should it decide?") is a decision framework disguised as a rhetorical device.
Viewer walks away with
A question they'll ask in their next meeting. High conviction, medium-term behaviour change — but only if it connects to something the viewer already cares about.
Risk
Inspirational but not actionable if the story doesn't land. Mismatch between speaker's personal stakes and audience's actual situation breaks the mirror.
STC use-case
Faculty/dean alignment. Accreditation committees. Partner/employer conversations. Any room where you need to shift a belief before presenting a plan.
Brandon Hancock

Learning by watching someone figure it out

"Show me struggling intelligently, then succeeding — and I'll believe I can too."

What it's doing
The live screen demo makes the learner a co-discoverer. Real errors, real terminal output, real debugging — the process is the content. Vague-vs-clear agent demo creates a comparison the viewer can reference later when writing their own instructions.
Core mechanism
Observational learning (Bandura). Seeing someone solve a problem in real time activates the same pathways as doing it yourself — the viewer mentally predicts what comes next. When they're wrong, the surprise deepens encoding.
Viewer walks away with
Permission and a template. "They did it, I can do it. I know roughly what my screen will look like." High near-transfer (same tool, slightly different task).
Risk
If the demo breaks badly or drags, engagement falls faster than any other format. Requires tight editing. Low abstraction = low far-transfer (different tool or context).
STC use-case
Current students. Developers already using AI tools. Follow-along labs. The "okay I want to actually build this" viewer segment after they've watched Vox or TED.
Malan / CS50

Discovery as ritual

"The moment of understanding should feel like something happened to you, not something you were told."

What it's doing
Physical props (envelopes, cards, volunteers) make the audience responsible for the reveal. The whiteboard tracks state visibly. The live audience reaction gives remote viewers a reference point for how to respond.
Core mechanism
Embodied cognition — manipulating physical objects while learning creates stronger encoding than watching or hearing alone. Active prediction (what will the card say?) means the learner is constructing the answer before it's given, making the reveal a confirmation rather than information delivery.
Viewer walks away with
A memory of the moment, not just the fact. High long-term retention. The envelope exercise can be replicated in a workshop, making it the most reproducible learning artefact of the four formats.
Risk
Requires a live or near-live production. Hardest to fake — a flat delivery of this format performs worse than a good Vox-style piece. The instructor's presence and energy are load-bearing.
STC use-case
Signature recorded lecture. New-student orientation. Faculty development workshops. Any context where you want the content to feel like a CityU/STC experience, not just internet content.

Learning Science Behind Each Format

The cognitive and pedagogical theories each format leans on

No format is accidental. Each one has drifted toward the techniques that work for its medium and audience over years of iteration. Here's what the research says about why they work.

Dual Coding (Paivio)

Information encoded in both verbal and visual channels is recalled more reliably. Vox maximises this — every concept has a paired animation. The other formats use it less deliberately.

Cognitive Load Theory (Sweller)

Working memory is limited. Vox offloads via animation. CS50 offloads via props and audience participation. TED and Brandon rely on the learner's working memory more — which is fine for motivated adults, risky for novices.

Narrative Transportation (Green & Brock)

When absorbed in a story, critical thinking drops and belief change rises. TED exploits this deliberately — the personal story at the open lowers the audience's defences before the argument is made.

Observational Learning (Bandura)

We learn from watching others succeed. Brandon's demo format works because the viewer isn't just watching a result — they're watching a process, including errors. Models who struggle and recover are more effective than models who perform flawlessly.

Desirable Difficulty (Bjork)

Learning that feels harder encodes deeper. CS50's prediction model (what will the card say?) is a built-in desirable difficulty — the learner generates an answer before seeing the correct one, which is more effective than passive reception.

Embodied Cognition (Lakoff & Johnson)

Physical actions during learning create stronger and more retrievable memories. Writing on the card at the end of Lecture 0 is not a cute prop trick — it's encoding the concept through motor memory, spatial location, and personal handwriting simultaneously.

Spacing & Interleaving (Ebbinghaus)

All four formats produce one dense learning event. To convert any of them into durable knowledge, they need follow-up: a lab, a discussion, a quiz, or a build task within 48 hours. The format gets them to the door; the curriculum has to take them through it.

Self-Efficacy (Bandura)

Belief that you can do something precedes actually doing it. Brandon's format builds self-efficacy directly ("I watched someone do it, I can picture myself doing it"). TED builds it indirectly through conviction. Vox builds curiosity but not confidence. CS50 builds ownership.

Decision Guide

Choose a format based on your constraints and goal

When you know your goal, your audience, and your production capacity — the format often chooses itself.

If your goal is… Best format Why
Maximum reach, social sharing Vox Fast, visually satisfying, repeatable as clips. High share-intent when the analogy lands.
Shift a belief or unlock buy-in TED Narrative transportation reduces resistance. The job-description question gives them something to use immediately.
Get someone to open a terminal Brandon Observational learning + self-efficacy boost. Seeing it done in real time removes the "I don't know where to start" blocker.
Create a memorable learning experience CS50 Embodied cognition + prediction + physical artefact = highest long-term retention of the four formats.
Low production capacity, async Brandon Screen recording + narration. No animation team, no stage, no audience needed. Highest ROI per production hour.
High production investment, flagship CS50 Vox CS50 for the signature recorded lecture; Vox for the accompanying explainer clips. Together they cover awareness + ownership.
Live event (conference, orientation) TED CS50 TED for the opening keynote slot; CS50 for the breakout/workshop. One shifts beliefs, the other creates the memory.
Recurring curriculum content CS50 The envelope exercise and whiteboard approach scale to any cohort and can be facilitated by any prepared instructor — not just the original creator.

Hybrid Approaches

Combining formats to cover more ground with fewer videos

The formats are not mutually exclusive. The most effective content series often borrow deliberately across them — using one format's strength to patch another's weakness.

The Awareness → Action sequence

Release the Vox-style explainer first to build awareness and social reach. Follow it within a week with the Brandon demo for people who are now curious enough to try it. The TED version can live as the "why it matters" piece for decision-makers who found the Vox piece too shallow.

Vox first → Brandon follow-up → TED for leaders

The Lecture + Lab pairing

Use CS50-style for the recorded lecture (high retention, high STC brand identity). Use Brandon-style for the companion lab walkthrough. Students watch the lecture to understand concepts, then watch the demo before their own coding session.

CS50 lecture + Brandon lab

The Workshop Opener

Open a live session with 3 minutes of a Vox-style clip to set context (works even for a non-technical audience). Then transition to CS50-style facilitation with props. The clip does the cognitive heavy lifting; the workshop does the embodied learning.

Vox clip (3 min) → CS50 workshop

The Borrow: TED opens a Brandon video

Start a screen-demo video with 90 seconds of personal story (TED device) before showing the terminal. This lifts the demo's emotional stakes — the viewer cares about the outcome before the code appears. Works especially well for "Why I switched to agents" tutorials.

TED open (90 sec) → Brandon demo

STC Recommendation

Which formats to prioritise given STC's goals and capacity

Given STC's position — a growing tech school with a flagship AI program, limited production budget, and three distinct audiences (leadership, faculty, students) — the format choice is a strategic asset allocation, not just a creative preference.

Priority order for "AI Agents, Explained"

1. CS50-style Lecture 0 (flagship). This is the one STC should invest in most. It creates a signature experience tied to the STC/CityU brand, scales to workshops and orientations, and produces the strongest long-term retention. A consistent on-camera anchor (named faculty or dean) over multiple episodes matters more than production polish — the CS50 comparison holds here.

2. Brandon-style demo (companion lab). High ROI, low production overhead. Pairs directly with the lecture to give students something to do. Release as the follow-up for every CS50 episode.

3. TED version (leadership brief). Use for the AAC presentation, faculty alignment sessions, and employer/partner conversations — not for YouTube. The job-description question is the single most portable tool in the series for non-technical decision-makers.

4. Vox/Kurzgesagt (when budget allows). The highest-reach format but the most expensive to produce well. Commission one or two animated pieces for the BSAI program launch — not as the primary curriculum delivery vehicle but as the top-of-funnel asset that drives enrollment conversations.
