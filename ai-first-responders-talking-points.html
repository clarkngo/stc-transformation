Presentation Scripts · 5-Min & 10-Min

The Impact of AI on
First Responders

Police officers, firefighters, and paramedics are already using AI on shift. These scripts present the research, the risks, and the path forward — for the STC President and the public.

🎯 Presented to: STC President ⏱ 5-min & 10-min versions 📅 June 2026 🖨 Print-ready

Start with a question — then the stakes

The Reality · ~1 min

Real wins — and real harm

⏱ 0:45 – 1:45
  • 1
    The upside is real. Axon Draft One: reports in 60 seconds. Longeye: 60 hours of digital evidence in under an hour. AI wildfire cameras: fires detected 25 minutes before the first 911 call. These tools save time and save lives.
  • 2
    But these tools are only about 80% accurate. That 20% error rate is showing up in official documents. An AI report in one department invented a full statement from an officer who did not exist. The officer signed it — immediate Brady material violation. The King County Prosecuting Attorney now refuses to accept AI-generated narratives in court. Full stop.
  • 3
    In EMS, an AI translation dropped the phrase "turning blue" from a 911 call. The paramedics arrived without knowing the patient was in respiratory distress. That's not a software glitch — that's a life-safety failure.
✅ 60s report writing
✅ 25 min early wildfire detection
🚨 ~20% AI error rate in reports
🚨 King County PA refuses AI narratives
Anchor: "The efficiency gains are real. The legal exposure is also real. Right now, first responders are carrying both — with no guidance on which is which."

The Framework · ~1 min

A traffic light for AI use — Green, Yellow, Red

⏱ 1:45 – 2:45
  • G
    Green — use it today: Fleet routing, bulk evidence processing with human review, translated public announcements. Low risk, high value. No new policy needed.
  • Y
    Yellow — pilot with supervision: Draft reports that require mandatory supervisor redlining before they go anywhere. Body camera summaries for training only. The AI drafts. A human owns it. Always.
  • R
    Red — hard stop: AI-written charging documents. Signing anything under penalty of perjury without full human verification. Running consumer AI on confidential case data. These aren't recommendations — they need to be policy.
Anchor: "This isn't about blocking AI. It's about making sure the people using it are protected."

The Urgency · ~45 sec

July 1 — the clock is running

⏱ 2:45 – 3:30
  • ▸
    AI tools are already being purchased ahead of any governance policy — locally in Redmond and nationally. Seattle's own AI Plan has a Pillar 3 mandating public safety workforce upskilling. It has not been implemented.
  • ▸
    Washington State's AI Task Force recommendations land July 1, 2026 — weeks away. Expected to require public disclosure of AI use and explicit officer attestation on every AI-assisted document. Departments that aren't ready will be scrambling.
Anchor: "The deadline isn't ours — it's the state's. We're either ahead of it or behind it."

Close · ~1 min

What needs to happen — and who it protects

The Ask

Three things that need attention

The research is done. The framework exists. What's needed now is institutional action — before the state deadline and before the next AI-generated error ends up in court.

  • Adopt the Green / Yellow / Red framework — give departments clear, actionable guidance on which AI uses are safe today, which need supervision, and which are banned.
  • Draft officer attestation language — with legal counsel and union input, so every AI-assisted document has a clear, legally defensible human sign-off process.
  • Deploy AI literacy training before July 1 — whether STC leads it, partners on it, or advocates for it, first responders need to know what these tools can and cannot do.

Section 2 · ~2 min

The risk — what ~80% accuracy actually means on the ground

⏱ 2:30 – 4:30
  • 1
    Ghost Officer Hallucination. An AI-generated police report included a full statement from an officer who did not exist. The officer signed it. That's a Brady material violation — the prosecution is legally required to hand that fabrication to the defense. Case potentially thrown out. Career potentially over.
  • 2
    Background noise misinterpretation. AI transcribed ambient noise as officer speech and produced a report claiming the suspect "shape-shifted into a frog." Unusable. Embarrassing. Potentially damaging if it ever reached court.
  • 3
    Medical translation failure — EMS dropped "turning blue" from a 911 translation. Paramedics arrived without knowing the patient was in respiratory distress. That is a life-safety failure, not a software inconvenience.
  • 4
    The King County Prosecuting Attorney has issued a formal memo — they will not accept AI-generated narratives in charging documents. Full stop. Brady material: any evidence favorable to the defendant that the prosecution must disclose by law. A fabricated AI statement qualifies — and can unravel an entire case.

The Ask — What Decision Are You Seeking?

Three things that need to happen before July 1

The research is done. The framework exists. The window before the state deadline is closing. These are the three actions that protect first responders, departments, and the public.

  • Adopt the Green / Yellow / Red framework — give departments actionable guidance immediately so officers know what is safe to use, what needs oversight, and what is off-limits.
  • Draft officer attestation language — with legal counsel and union input, create clear, defensible language so every AI-assisted document has an explicit human sign-off process before the July 1 state mandate.
  • Deploy AI literacy training across SPD, SFD, and EMS — whether STC leads it, partners on it, or advocates for it, first responders need to understand what these tools can and cannot do before the next AI error ends up in court.
