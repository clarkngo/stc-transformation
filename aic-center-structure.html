The Center Liaison is the connective tissue. Without this role, the pipeline between STC faculty, students, and active Center engagements depends on informal relationships — and breaks under workload pressure. The Liaison manages release time, practicum placements, and curriculum feedback loops between both units.
Start lean. Year 1 is one Director, the Liaison, and faculty fellows from STC. SMEs are brought in per engagement — not hired full-time until the pipeline justifies it.

How STC and the Center Work Together

STC stays intact. The Center draws from it — it doesn't absorb it.

School of Technology & Computing

  • Owns degree programs, curriculum, and academic standards
  • Hires and retains all faculty — no faculty report to the Center
  • Faculty participate in Center projects via release time, negotiated through the Liaison
  • Student enrollment and advising stays within STC
  • Gets first look at engagement findings to update curriculum
  • Center Liaison role created within STC — reports to STC Dean, not Center Director
⇄
Liaison

Applied Intelligence Center

  • Owns client relationships, engagements, and deliverables
  • Requests faculty fellows and student cohorts through the Liaison — not directly
  • Controls its own budget and revenue — separate from STC financials
  • Brings SMEs in per project without STC approval needed
  • Micro-credentials built with STC faculty input but branded as Center products
  • Director reports to the President's Office — not to STC Dean
The boundary that matters most: The Center Director does not have direct authority over STC faculty time. All faculty assignments go through the Liaison. This protects faculty from being pulled in two directions and prevents resentment that would undermine the whole model.

Consulting is the engine in Year 1. It funds operations, builds credibility, and generates the case studies that make micro-credentials sellable. Grants follow once there's a track record.
Year 1 — Build

Consulting-Led

Project-Based Consulting SPD pilot as anchor engagement. Port and sports as secondary targets. Fee-for-service, scoped deliverables.
Micro-Credentials (Prep) No revenue yet — curriculum design begins using engagement IP. Practitioner-publisher partners identified.
Grants (Research) Identify DOJ, DHS, NSF public safety AI grant opportunities. First applications drafted.
Year 2 — Diversify

Two-Stream

Consulting (Expanded) 2–3 active engagements. SPD model replicated for port and sports. Retainer conversations begin with repeat clients.
Micro-Credentials (Launch) First 2–3 short courses live on Open edX. Co-developed with practitioner-publishers from STC ecosystem. Revenue split defined.
Grants (First Awards) First grant funding received, backed by SPD pilot case study as proof of impact.
Year 3 — Sustain

Mixed & Self-Sustaining

Consulting Core revenue stream. 4–6 engagements annually. Some multi-year retainer relationships.
Micro-Credentials Growing catalog. Community and cohort models generating recurring revenue independent of consulting activity.
Grants Active grant portfolio. Funds research staff and student fellowships without drawing from consulting revenue.
IP & Licensing Frameworks, tools, and risk registries developed during engagements — packaged and licensed to peer institutions or agencies.

Year 1 → Year 3 Maturity Model

What the Center looks like at each stage — people, engagements, and market position
Year 1 — Prove It

One Engagement. One Team. One Win.

  • Director (internal) + Liaison in place
  • SPD pilot as sole active engagement
  • 2–3 STC faculty fellows on the project
  • First student practicum cohort (4–6 students)
  • 1–2 external SMEs brought in for SPD vendor analysis
  • Micro-credential curriculum design begins
  • First grant applications submitted
  • Center identity and website launched
Year 2 — Scale It

Multiple Engagements. Proven Model.

  • SPD engagement extended or replicated
  • Port Authority and one sports org engaged
  • SME roster grows to 4–6 recurring experts
  • First micro-credentials live — 2–3 courses
  • Practicum cohort expands to 10–15 students
  • First grant funding received
  • Director begins transition plan toward external hire
  • Revenue covers Center operating costs
Year 3 — Lead It

Regional Hub for Applied AI & Cybersecurity.

  • External Director hired — industry credibility, national network
  • 4–6 active engagements across public and private sectors
  • Growing micro-credential catalog with practitioner-publisher co-authors
  • Grant portfolio funds dedicated research and student fellowships
  • Risk registry and frameworks licensed to peer institutions
  • Center recognized as the Pacific Northwest's go-to for public sector AI governance
  • STC curriculum directly shaped by Center engagement outputs
The Year 1 anchor is SPD. A successful pilot — with a university-authored audit report the department can take to command staff — is the case study that opens every door after it. Port, sports, other municipalities. The pilot isn't just one engagement; it's the proof of concept for the entire Center model.
