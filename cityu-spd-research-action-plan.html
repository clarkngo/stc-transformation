Working Thesis

What we now know changes the pitch

Don't lead with
Time savings on report narratives. An independent, pre-registered RCT (Adams et al., 2024) found no significant reduction in report-writing time from Axon's Draft One — directly contradicting vendor marketing. Loren Atherley raised this study unprompted, which means SPD's own research arm is already skeptical of the efficiency pitch. Leading with "2 hours → 60 seconds" undercuts our credibility with the one audience whose trust we need most.
Lead with instead
What SPD is already doing: positioning AI as QA, consistency, and supervisory accountability tooling (Mark 43 prompts, planned NLP review of reasonable-suspicion language) rather than a narrative-writing shortcut. This is also the approach least likely to trip the King County Prosecutor's blanket prohibition, since it never puts AI output into a signed narrative.
CityU's actual opening
Not "build us an AI report writer." It's research partner, third-party evaluator, and education provider — three roles SPD explicitly said it doesn't have covered (no evaluation partner since PNNL lost funding; no internal capacity to run the kind of mixed tech + social-science evaluation Loren described wanting).

Prosecutor Strategy

The blanket AI prohibition is the central blocker — treat it as political, not technical

Diagnosis
Group consensus on the call: the prohibition is preemptive of public concern, not a response to a specific incident. Root cause still unconfirmed — needs to be established before any messaging is built.
Approach
  • Don't lecture the prosecutor's office — bring them along.
  • Separate "AI" from "technology" in the conversation. A lot of the QA/transcription work isn't generative AI at all; conflating them invites unnecessary opposition.
  • Demonstrate trust incrementally with already-running tools (Mark 43, CaseX/Corvus) before reopening the narrative-writing question.
  • Use Jai Stone's interviews (see above) to find the actual lever — is it case-loss risk, liability exposure, or general caution?
Reference point
See ref-7 in the Research Brief for the EFF coverage of the underlying King County PA memo, and the brief's broader framing of why AI-as-QA is more defensible than AI-as-author.

Research Presentation Framework & Guardrails

How CityU presents evidence — so we don't get caught out by our own slides

What we learned
In the June 16 meeting, a vendor-derived "99.2% faster" time-savings claim was on our slide when SPD's own research scientist had a peer-reviewed RCT in hand that found no significant time savings. We didn't lose the room — Clark immediately acknowledged the counter-study and Loren's framing actually aligned with ours — but it exposed a gap in how we vet and label the evidence behind our claims before a meeting. This framework closes that gap.
Step 1 — Tier every claim before it goes on a slide

Every data point we present should be tagged with one of three tiers. If you can't tag it, it doesn't go in the deck.

Tier 1 — Independent research
Peer-reviewed / RCT / government study

Can be stated as fact. Still requires context (sample size, agency type, recency). Example: Adams et al. 2024 RCT on report-writing speed.

Tier 2 — Applied / practitioner evidence
Agency pilots, conference reports, anecdotal from practitioners

Can be stated as "field experience suggests…" Always name the source. Example: Chiefs at PERF said officers liked using it even without speed gains.

Tier 3 — Vendor-provided
Marketing claims, press releases, internal studies

Must be labeled as vendor claims. Never present as research. Always pair with independent evidence or acknowledge the gap. Example: "Axon claims 82% reduction — not yet independently verified."

Step 2 — When evidence is contested, own the tension

The worst outcome is being caught defending a claim you didn't vet. The best outcome is being the people in the room who already know both sides.

❌ Don't say

"AI reduces report writing time from 2 hours to 60 seconds."

This is a Tier 3 vendor claim stated as fact. If your audience has read Adams et al., you've lost credibility before the conversation starts.

✅ Say instead

"Vendors claim up to 82% time reduction. The first independent RCT found no significant difference — but it only measured narrative writing, not full report time or quality. That gap is exactly what we're designed to evaluate."

❌ Don't say

"AI tools are 80% accurate."

This is an aggregate figure from mixed sources with no agency or tool context. In a room with a research scientist, it will be challenged immediately.

✅ Say instead

"Accuracy varies significantly by tool, use case, and how 'accuracy' is defined. Hallucinations are documented and real — which is why human-in-the-loop validation is non-negotiable, not optional."

Step 3 — Pre-meeting guardrails checklist

Run every deck through this before entering a room with SPD, legal, or the prosecutor's office.

1️⃣
Is every statistic tagged with a source tier? If a number has no citation or is from a vendor press release, it either gets labeled as such or comes out.
2️⃣
Do we know the counter-argument for every Tier 2 or Tier 3 claim? If there's a published study that disputes a claim we're making, we should be the ones who bring it up — not the audience. Citing the counter-study ourselves is a credibility gain, not a concession.
3️⃣
Is the recency of each study noted? AI moves fast. A 2022 study on GPT-3 accuracy is not a claim about Claude 3.7 or GPT-4o. Always note the year and model generation when citing AI performance research.
4️⃣
Are we leading with what we know vs. what we're proposing to find out? CityU's value to SPD is as evaluators and researchers — not as people who have all the answers already. It's fine (and often stronger) to say "we don't know yet, and that's the study we want to run."
5️⃣
Have we separated what the evidence says from what we recommend? These are two different things and should never be collapsed. "The research shows X" and "we therefore recommend Y" need to be clearly distinct sentences. Recommendations are ours to own; findings belong to the study authors.
6️⃣
Is there a Tier 3 claim carrying weight it shouldn't? If removing the vendor number would collapse the argument, the argument isn't ready. The case should stand on Tier 1 and Tier 2 evidence, with Tier 3 presented only as context for what's being claimed in the market.
Step 4 — If challenged in the room

What happened on June 16 was actually the right response — Clark acknowledged the counter-study, noted the recency gap, and kept moving. That's the playbook.

  • Acknowledge immediately. "That's the Adams study — yes, we've read it. Here's what we think it does and doesn't tell us…"
  • Narrow the claim, don't abandon it. "The speed finding is disputed. The quality and consistency findings aren't — and that's actually the more important argument for this audience."
  • Redirect to what we're there to do. "This is exactly why independent evaluation matters. You shouldn't have to take vendor numbers or our numbers on faith — you should have a research partner who runs the test."
The meta-rule: CityU's credibility in this partnership comes from being the people who read the fine print — not from having the most optimistic numbers. Every time we acknowledge a limitation before the room does, we bank trust. Every time the room catches us on something we should have known, we spend it.
