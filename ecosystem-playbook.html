Making the Ecosystem Pluggable

Framework vs. platform — and a starter kit either way
❓

The Problem

Why this matters
The practitioner-publisher ecosystem model — faculty/industry experts sharing free content to build trust before marketing degrees — worked for STC's Blue Tech and First Responders tracks. But right now it only exists as something STC built for itself. Jen's question: how do we build it so other departments can plug in, instead of STC re-explaining and rebuilding it for every school that wants to try it?
⚖️

Framework vs. Platform

Framework — a method
  • Documented playbook, checklists, and templates another department follows on its own.
  • Low build cost — mostly writing and packaging what STC already learned.
  • Each department runs its own content, own channels, own cadence.
  • Risk: quality and consistency drift department to department with no shared infrastructure.
Platform — shared infrastructure
  • Shared tooling: a content calendar, publishing hub, or analytics dashboard other schools use directly.
  • Higher build cost — needs product ownership, not just documentation.
  • Consistent measurement and branding across departments by construction.
  • Risk: over-building before demand is proven; becomes another IT project to maintain.
Recommendation: start as a framework, evolve pieces into a platform only where a second and third department hit the same bottleneck. This mirrors the "discovery, development, and delivery" phasing already used for AILI and the maritime research — don't build shared infrastructure before more than one department has proven the method works for them.
🧰

Starter Kit — What Another Department Needs on Day One

📋 Practitioner-Publisher Role Guide

One page: what the role is, why it works pre-inquiry, and 2-3 examples (e.g. the 18-second GitHub tutorial at ~180K views).

🧭 Community-Need Discovery Checklist

How to ground a new content track in real mandates — the same approach used for the Seattle AI plan and Coast Guard cybersecurity mandates in maritime research.

🚦 Traffic-Light Risk Worksheet

The green/yellow/red framework from the SPD AI pilot, adapted as a generic "yes, and" risk-mitigation tool for any new department initiative.

📈 Minimum Measurement Template

A lightweight way to track whether content is actually building a pre-inquiry funnel (views, saves, inbound questions) without needing a full analytics platform.

This needs an answer before a second department is onboarded, so ownership isn't decided ad hoc under time pressure.

The Cooking-Show Briefing Template

Lead with the finished dish, then unpack the ingredients
🍳

The Recipe

  1. 1
    The finished dish
    Open with the outcome — a stat, demo, or claim the audience can react to immediately (e.g. "police reports: 2 hours to 60 seconds"). No setup, no agenda slide first.
  2. 2
    The ingredients
    Now name the components that built it, one at a time — each one lands as "oh, that's how" instead of "wait, why are we talking about this."
  3. 3
    The recipe
    Show how the ingredients combine — the method, sequencing, or tradeoffs (e.g. how the "yes, and" mindset and the traffic-light framework work together to manage risk).
  4. 4
    Plating notes
    Close with what's next — the ask, the open question, or the decision needed from the room.
🔁

Worked Example — Applying It to the July 1 Roundtable

Section used
AI for First Responders (SPD Pilot) — reworked below in cooking-show order.
Traditional order (build-up)
  1. Background on SPD's report-writing burden
  2. Risks: audio transcription accuracy
  3. The "yes, and" mitigation approach
  4. The traffic-light framework
  5. Result: 2 hrs → 60 sec draft time
Cooking-show order
  1. Finished dish: 2 hrs → 60 sec draft time
  2. Ingredients: audio-transcription risk, "yes, and" mindset, traffic-light framework
  3. Recipe: how yes-and + traffic-light let the pilot move despite the risk
  4. Plating: ask — approve the pilot scope
✅

Checklist for the Next Briefing

  • First slide/sentence states the outcome, not the agenda.
  • Every "ingredient" introduced after the outcome ties visibly back to it.
  • The "recipe" step explains how the pieces combine — not just a list of parts.
  • Closing line is a specific ask or decision, not a summary recap.
