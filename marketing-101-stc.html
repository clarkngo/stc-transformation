Marketing is not only ads. In a school of technology and computing, marketing is the disciplined work of making value, proof, and next steps obvious — first to colleagues and partners who must carry the message, then to students and employers in the open market. This page names that sequence so your content engine, program launches, and classroom stories stay aligned.

Why technical people pick up marketing faster than they expect

Most canonical frameworks are structured decision-making under different vocabulary. Market segmentation is close to data clustering (who clusters together on needs, constraints, and readiness). Positioning is feature differentiation in the buyer’s mental model, not only in your spec sheet. The customer funnel is a conversion pipeline with drop-off at each stage — the same intuition you use for systems throughput.

The mental shift that still hurts

Marketing forces you to reason from the customer’s perception outward, not from the product specification inward. A technically superior program with weak positioning consistently loses to a weaker competitor with clear, credible positioning — because buyers optimize for risk reduction and comprehension speed, not raw feature count. That lesson is the hardest for engineers and pure-academic leaders to internalize; naming it early saves bruised egos in cross-functional meetings.

For a Dean of a School of Technology

The other tabs highlight the four frameworks that pay rent fastest in your role: STP (who to recruit and what to say to each segment), value proposition canvas (why your stack beats Coursera, bootcamps, and peer institutions for a defined job-to-be-done), customer journey (including alumni as the top of a new funnel), and LTV:CAC (enrollment investment framed in unit economics for finance committees). Diagrams: open the Value prop canvas, Customer journey, and Funnel & mix tabs for downloadable SVG figures.

STP — segmentation, targeting, positioning

Segmentation: partition the market into groups that respond similarly to an offer (working adults, career switchers, employer-sponsored cohorts, international, online-only vs hybrid, etc.). Targeting: choose which segments you will serve first given capacity, accreditation, and margin. Positioning: the deliberate story and proof you attach to the chosen segments — what you want them to repeat about you in one sentence.

  • Use different messages per segment while keeping one truth spine (outcomes, assessments, support model) so advisors and web do not contradict each other.
  • Explicitly decide what you will not chase this cycle; it sharpens the target and protects faculty load.

Value proposition canvas (Osterwalder-style)

Pair customer jobs, pains, and gains with your program’s pain relievers, gain creators, and products/services. For a tech school, the “competition” in the canvas is not only other universities: it includes self-study (Coursera, Udemy), bootcamps, employer academies, and “do nothing / stay in current job.”

  • Force one row on risk reversal (time to first credential, transfer policy clarity, trial course, employer pathway).
  • Align “gains” to measurable outcomes you already publish or can defend in accreditation language.

Customer journey (and alumni as funnel top)

Map stages from first touch through enrollment, persistence, graduation, and alumni. Alumni success is not only “welfare” — it is marketing infrastructure: referrals, employer intros, guest lectures, donor stories, and social proof for the next cohort.

  • Instrument a few journey metrics per stage (e.g. time-to-advisor reply, % receiving faculty welcome, capstone showcase attendance).
  • Design one alumni touch per year that feeds both advancement and recruiting narratives without overburdening graduates.

LTV : CAC — unit economics for enrollment

CAC is total recruitment + marketing + admissions touch cost divided by new starts (define the denominator with finance). LTV is discounted net tuition and auxiliary revenue over expected enrollment length, minus service cost — simplified models are fine for directional committee conversations if assumptions are labeled.

  • Framing enrollment as unit economics helps align marketing spend, scholarship policy, and program mix with the same vocabulary the CFO uses.
  • Never publish internal LTV:CAC without governance; use ranges and footnoted assumptions externally.

Funnel, AIDA, and extended 7Ps

AIDA (attention → interest → desire → action) is the classic narrative arc for creative; pair it with operational metrics at each transition. The 4Ps / 7Ps still help checklists for program launches: add people, process, physical evidence for schools because service delivery is the product.

  • Match top-of-funnel content (thought leadership, events) to middle-funnel proof (syllabus excerpts, faculty bios, outcomes) before you buy bottom-funnel clicks.
  • See also this hub’s Marketing 101 and Content Engine for attribution discipline.

                01
                

                    
Marketing 101 (higher-ed and STEM lens)

                    
Definitions that survive a faculty meeting

                

            

            
Marketing is choosing who you serve, what promise you can keep, and how people discover, believe, and act on that promise. Communications is the craft of packaging that promise in channels (slides, email, web, events, media). Brand is the accumulated memory of kept promises.

            

                

                    
Classic frame (still useful)

                    
Offer & proof

                    
Product (programs, certificates, labs), Price (tuition, time, opportunity cost), Place (online, hybrid, partner sites), Promotion (everything visible). In STC, add People (faculty, advisors, alumni mentors), Process (registration, support, grading integrity), and Physical evidence (syllabi, tooling, capstone artifacts).

                

                

                    
Funnel without jargon

                    
Awareness → trust → action

                    
Someone hears of CityU STC → compares options → talks to a human or reads deep content → applies or joins a list. Marketing’s job is to remove friction and doubt at each hop, not to shout louder at the top.

                

            

            

                Non-negotiable in tech education: claims must be tethered to outcomes, outcomes to assessments, assessments to faculty capacity. Marketing that outruns delivery creates refunds, bad reviews, and accreditation risk.
            

        


        

            

                02
                

                    
What “marketing” means inside STC

                    
Programs, employers, students, and the wider university

                

            

            
The School of Technology and Computing markets multiple overlapping offers: degrees and certificates, career pathways into tech roles, faculty expertise (research and applied), and employer relevance (advisory boards, projects, hiring pipelines). Each audience needs a different emphasis but one consistent spine so recruiters, deans, and faculty do not contradict each other.

            
    
                
  • Prospective students want clarity on outcomes, time-to-degree, support, and how AI and software skills show up in courses — not slogan stacks.
    • 
                
  • Current students are marketed to as well: retention is internal marketing (belonging, advising, guilds, transparent grading).
    • 
                
  • Employers & partners want signal on graduate capabilities, project depth, and low-friction ways to engage (guest lectures, capstone, apprenticeships).
    • 
                
  • University leadership & advancement need proof points that ladder to institutional priorities (completion, innovation, equity, revenue mix).
    • 
            

            
Cross-link: map program stories to pillars on the strategic alignment page so every external claim has an internal “owner pillar.”

        


        

            

                03
                

                    
Internal marketing first

                    
Alignment before amplification

                

            

            
Internal marketing is the work of winning informed advocates inside the school and across CityU: chairs, advisors, registrars, marketing & comms, advancement, and faculty who will repeat your story in their own networks. If they are surprised by the public website, you skipped this step.

            

                

                    
Artifacts

                    
What to produce early

                    
One-page message house (audience, promise, three proof points, call-to-action), FAQ for advisors, slide skeleton for info sessions, and a change log when curriculum or policy shifts so comms stays accurate.

                

                

                    
Rituals

                    
How it shows up

                    
Short briefings before public launches, shared language in LMS templates, “office hours” for staff questions, and pilot cohorts whose feedback becomes testimonial and process fixes before broad promotion.

                

            

            
Internal marketing lowers the cost of external campaigns: fewer retractions, fewer angry threads, faster approvals, and higher-quality student-staff interactions on day one.

        


        

            

                04
                

                    
Pitch and communication discipline

                    
Structures that survive scrutiny

                

            

            
A pitch is not charisma; it is compressed logic. For STC contexts, use a repeatable spine:

            
    
                
  • Problem — skills gap, career transition, or employer need (one sentence, concrete).
    • 
                
  • Audience — who this program is for — and who it is not for (reduces mismatched enrollments).
    • 
                
  • Proof — outcomes, assessments, faculty credentials, student artifacts, partners (prefer numbers or named examples you can defend).
    • 
                
  • Mechanism — how learning happens (stack, projects, support), not buzzwords.
    • 
                
  • Ask — apply, RSVP, join list, sponsor a capstone — one primary ask per touchpoint.
    • 
            

            

                

                    
Async

                    
Email & docs

                    
BLUF (bottom line up front), scannable headings, links to one canonical page per initiative, and explicit owners for replies. Avoid duplicating policy in email — link to source of truth.

                

                

                    
Sync

                    
Meetings & talks

                    
Agenda with decisions needed, time-boxed Q&A, and a single shared deck version. For mixed audiences (faculty + marketing), separate academic detail slides from public narrative slides in the same file using hidden sections or appendices.

                

            

        


        

            

                05
                

                    
How internal work becomes external marketing

                    
A clean handoff

                

            

            
When the message house and advisor FAQ are stable, external marketing is mostly distribution and creative: web pages, paid media, events, PR, and partner co-marketing. The STC Content Engine brief describes how technical thought leadership feeds the top of the funnel with measurable attribution.

            

                Internal alignment (shared story + proof + constraints)

                → Pitch kit (slides, one-pagers, talk track)

                → Canonical web & CRM assets (single URLs, forms, UTM discipline)

                → Public campaigns (content, ads, events) that quote the same proof advisors already use.
            


            
Democratization thought leadership

            
In content strategy, what you are describing has a recognizable pattern: democratization thought leadership. The school claims authority not by out-deepening specialist competitors (for example, trying to out-explain a developer-grade ADK masterclass), but by going wider — opening the same ideas to everyone those specialists report to, collaborate with, and pitch to: managers, policy makers, educators, clinical and operations leaders, and partner organizations. The adjacent audience is materially larger than the core practitioner market alone. The STC Content Engine brief is the operational layer; this is the positioning logic behind why that layer includes non-code explainers alongside technical bridge content.

            
Why a simple agent example anchors the story. A deliberately small agent (for example the “greeting” / first agent in Example #1 — video plan) is a strong teaching anchor because it carries the philosophical questions non-technical stakeholders actually ask: What does the agent seem to “want”? Who authored its tone and boundaries? What breaks when instructions are vague or wrong? What happens if the goal is mis-specified? You do not need a terminal window to run that conversation — a workplace scenario and a plain-language walkthrough are often more memorable for the audiences above.

            

                Primary execution risk: the anchor persona. Flagship lecture-style courses often become inseparable from one consistent human face on camera over years (think of how CS50 is associated with a single instructor). For STC, that role needs a clear owner: typically the Dean as the recurring voice, or a designated faculty partner who can carry the same commitment. Production polish, channel mix, and credentialing all matter — but they are secondary to sustained presence and a single credible narrator the market learns to recognize.
            

            
On this hub, the Lecture 0 (lecture-hall) and Episode 1 (explainer / TED / screen-demo) scripts are concrete format experiments for that wider lane — not substitutes for faculty review of claims, but aligned with the same handoff discipline in the bullets above.


            
If external copy introduces a new superlative that internal teams cannot explain in plain language, pause and rewrite. That mismatch is how institutions lose trust.

                07
                

                    
Case studies (composite, tied to this hub)

                    
Patterns you can reuse — not anonymous vendor fairy tales

                

            

            
These three vignettes are structured as case studies (situation → action → result) and deliberately anchored to documents already on this site so you can drill into evidence.

            

                

                    Case A · Flagship program
                    
From approval to coherent recruiting narrative

                    
Situation: A new degree needs one story for leadership, advisors, and public channels without overpromising on AI job markets.

                    
Action: Build the message house from the approved outcomes and course stack; train advisors on boundaries (“what we teach” vs. “what employers vary by region”).

                    
Result: Public pages and partner decks cite the same outcomes; internal reviewers catch drift early. Source pack: BSAI AAC approved program, feedback synthesis.

                    
Proof anchor: program outcomes + course list in the approved submission.

                

                

                    Case B · Internal evidence
                    
Turning analytics into a defensible story

                    
Situation: Marketing wants “strong foundations” copy; faculty want claims tied to performance.

                    
Action: Pair narrative with aggregated course metrics (transparent scope: cohorts, time window) and avoid cherry-picking single sections.

                    
Result: Recruiting language that survives faculty Q&A. Source: Program analytics & enrollment metrics (e.g. foundational course rollups summarized there).

                    
Example fields on hub: total enrollments across tracked foundational courses, average GPA, at-risk counts — use only with the same definitions as the source page.

                

                

                    Case C · Community / retention
                    
Marketing belonging, not only acquisition

                    
Situation: High early engagement but club churn risks pipeline narratives.

                    
Action: Treat the guild model as an internal product: roles, cadence, faculty sponsorship — then publish student-visible value props.

                    
Result: External storytelling (tours, web) matches what students actually experience. Sources: Guild proposal, framework.

                    
Measure retention proxies: event attendance, repeat term participation, mentor ratios (define before you publish).

                

            

        


        

            

                08
                

                    
Scenarios (tabletop drills)

                    
15-minute team exercises

                

            

            

                Scenario 1 — “The chancellor forwarded the headline.”
                A blog title overstates job guarantees. Who owns the correction? Draft a 120-word internal note and a 80-word public revision. Rule: no new promises in the fix — only alignment to documented outcomes.
            

            

                Scenario 2 — Conflicting advisor scripts.
                Two advisors describe different math prerequisites. Build a 5-bullet FAQ sourced from the catalog and program guide; pick one canonical link for email templates.
            

            

                Scenario 3 — Partner wants co-branded “certificate inside a degree.”
                Internal marketing: map IP, accreditation, and transcript notation. External: nothing goes live until legal and academic affairs sign the same paragraph.
            

            

                Scenario 4 — Viral student TikTok misstates a course.
                Internal: faculty response + student comms. External: optional clarifier post; avoid arguing with comments — point to canonical page.
