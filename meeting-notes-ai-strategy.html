AI for First Responders & Law Enforcement

Efficiency
AI can dramatically shrink the time it takes officers to write reports — from two hours down to 60 seconds — and can review terabytes of digital video evidence that would otherwise require manual review.
Risks
AI usage carries significant operational and legal risks, including a current 20% error rate and the potential for "ghost officer hallucinations" — embellished or fictitious scenes inserted into AI-drafted reports. If an officer signs such a report, it could result in Brady material exposure, costing them their job and compromising the prosecutor's case. Additional concerns include difficulty transcribing background noise and biases embedded in historical training data.
Framework
Traffic Light Framework proposed to govern AI use:
Green

Approved for back-end processing — automated tasks without officer-facing output.

Yellow

Prototyping allowed with a human in the loop to review and verify outputs.

Red

Strictly banned — unvetted consumer AI tools and AI-generated initial charging documents.

The "Practitioner-Publisher" Educational Ecosystem

Faculty
The university is shifting toward a "practitioner-publisher" model, recruiting external industry experts who already have established digital brands, content libraries, and followings on platforms like YouTube or GitHub — rather than relying solely on traditional academic hires.
Pedagogy
This ecosystem prioritizes hands-on, step-by-step competency learning over traditional academic concepts, filling a gap for non-STEM or struggling students who need applied skill-building rather than theory-first instruction.
Delivery
Courses and micro-credentials will be hosted on flexible platforms like Open edX and GitHub rather than Coursera — avoiding high licensing costs while retaining the university's own brand identity and data.
Marketing
Modern students often bypass traditional university inquiry phases, relying instead on AI tools and search engines to evaluate programs. Partnering with existing content creators functions as a thought leadership and marketing strategy — driving organic traffic, building community hubs, and generating alternative revenue through micro-credentials.

Future Consulting Opportunities & Partnerships

🚔 Seattle Police Department

The department is seeking AI tools to draft reports from body-worn cameras, reducing officer desk time. The university is considering acting as an external technical consultant to evaluate software vendors, navigate AI restrictions, and build a risk registry to mitigate AI bias and legal exposure.

⚓ Maritime / Port Authority

A maritime expert requested a briefing on the team's "blue tech" research. Following a recent cyber attack at the port, there is an immediate opening to introduce the university's cybersecurity and AI expertise as a consulting resource.

🏈 Professional Sports Teams

Conversations are underway with local professional sports organizations facing cybersecurity challenges. The university's new ecosystem could provide tech team upskilling and consulting across multiple franchises.

💡 Consulting Model

Across all three verticals, the university's role is evaluator and risk navigator — not vendor — helping organizations build governance frameworks, assess software, and manage emerging AI liability.

Institutional Restructuring

Rebranding
To properly house this agile, non-traditional consulting and micro-credential model, the current structure of the School of Technology and Computing is under review. A dedicated "Center" with a new name is being explored — one that signals a clear departure from traditional higher education structures and positions the university as an applied innovation hub.
Open question: What name and structure best differentiates the Center from conventional academic departments while maintaining institutional credibility?
