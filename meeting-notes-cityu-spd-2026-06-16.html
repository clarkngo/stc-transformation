Context & Goal

Background
CityU has been researching "AI for first responders" broadly, looking at commonalities across police, fire, and EMS — not just SPD. The original catalyst was Chief Barnes's request to explore AI for incident report writing.
Chief Barnes's stated goals
Leverage whatever AI capability current policy allows; increase officer experience; decrease time spent on administrative tasks; put more officer time back into the community.
📊

Key Data Point Presented (Clark's Research)

Time savings claim
Average police report currently takes ~2 hours to write (officer pulled out of the field). Goal floated: get a first AI-assisted draft down to ~60 seconds.
Accuracy caveat
Cited AI tool accuracy figures around 80%, not 100% — group agreed the priority is identifying and mitigating risk, plus having a plan for when something still goes wrong.
Counterpoint raised by Loren Atherley: a 2024 peer-reviewed study (Adams et al., Journal of Experimental Criminology) on Axon's "Draft One" found no statistically significant time savings from AI-assisted report writing in a randomized controlled trial — directly contradicting vendor marketing claims of an 82% reduction. Loren is sourcing the article for the group; Clark noted the study is already ~2 years old and newer models may perform differently.
🚫

Major Obstacle: King County Prosecutor

Policy
Hard, blanket prohibition on AI involvement in anything an officer writes, especially narratives — no AI-assisted first drafts allowed at all.
Diagnosis
Group consensus: this is a political position, not grounded in a specific incident — likely preemptive of public concern rather than evidence-based. Root cause ("the why") still needs to be pinned down before crafting a response.
Possible workarounds discussed
  • Position AI as QA/copy-editor, not author — officer writes in own words, AI checks against policy requirements ("you're missing X").
  • Microsoft's approach: AI drafts a narrative from officer prompts but deliberately inserts odd/nonsensical words, forcing the officer to re-read, correct, validate, and sign off.
  • Use AI to compare body-worn-camera transcript against the written report for QA, rather than having AI write the report.
  • Strategy from Jai Stone: "You only need ONE successful integration to open the gates." Slow-walk adoption, build trust in one low-risk use case before expanding ("crawl, walk, run," not "swing for the fences").
🛠️

What SPD Already Has in Motion

📝 CaseX / Corvus

AI-assisted public-facing online crime reporting tool, just cleared privacy review. Walks the public through report creation, diverts non-crime reports, and drafts a report the citizen reviews and approves before submitting.

✅ Mark 43 QA utility

RMS vendor's new prompt-based quality assurance feature — checks completeness (e.g., verifying a hospital is listed when an officer says someone was transported). Just submitted for privacy approval.

🔎 Planned NLP review model

Flags poorly articulated reasonable suspicion / probable cause distinctions for supervisor review — a training and accountability tool, not a report-drafting tool.

🤖 Closed Claude instance

City has procured a private/closed Claude instance specifically for SPD (separate from city-wide Copilot), with guardrails and an SPD-specific knowledge base via MCP. Currently in low-risk use case phase (public data only) while security/roles/permissions are configured; medium/high-risk phases planned next. MCP configuration permission was granted the morning of this meeting — Claude expected online for SPD within the next couple of weeks.

Policy status
AI policy draft has been reviewed by Legal and Privacy; currently sitting with the policy unit for standard review. Strict internal rule: employees may not enter CJIS / PII / other protected data into any generative AI system — the core reason for building an in-house, closed instance rather than using a public cloud model.
Open question
How do other police/sheriff departments handle CJIS data in AI-assisted report writing, given SPD officers currently cannot bring CJIS data into any GenAI tool? Unresolved — flagged for follow-up.

Other Ideas Raised

Alt. low-risk use case
Reduce field training officer documentation time (currently 2 hrs of overtime/day) as a lower-risk AI pilot, separate from patrol report writing.
Citizen-facing accuracy
Could AI help community members file more accurate self-reports, reducing the rework/correction rate SPD currently sees?
Research partnership
Loren noted SPD has a robust global research network (criminology + tech) and is open to a formal MOU with CityU. CityU's research approach is distinguished as applied and consumable, versus purely peer-reviewed academic output.
Evaluation role
CityU offered to serve as a third-party evaluator — combining technologists and social scientists so findings aren't dismissed as purely techno-optimist or exposed to bias critiques. CityU could also interview internal "skeptics" (e.g., the prosecutor's office) to surface root objections. Loren separately noted SPD doesn't yet have an evaluation partner and is looking for help pursuing ISO certification (preferred over NIST) for the closed Claude implementation — a prior candidate, PNNL, lost funding last year.
Framework
Jai Stone's model: problem → process → people → platform. She also framed a "four-headed dragon" needing attention: advocacy (top-down), integration (policy-down), education (leadership / workforce / policy), and bias.
External interest
Lee Hunt noted Microsoft recently held an AI public-safety workshop and is interested in assisting SPD.
Budget angle
Chief Barnes raised a political/budget argument: AI report-writing efficiency could reduce officer overtime costs. He's not pursuing a "fewer officers needed" framing until SPD returns to full staffing.
