Overview

Summary
The Roundtable synthesized several strategic themes for CityU's Blue Tech & AI initiatives: how to weigh risk against opportunity in frontline-AI pilots (e.g., police report writing), how to ground new program discovery in real community and legislative need, and how to reach prospective students earlier through open, trust-building content rather than direct marketing.
💡

Lessons Learned

Lesson 1
Embrace a "yes, and" mindset for risk mitigation. The university is shifting away from a culture where listing concerns leads to paralysis. Leaders are adopting a "yes, and" approach — acknowledging risks while simultaneously figuring out how to mitigate them so projects can move forward. Example: exploring AI for police report writing, the team identified severe risks (e.g., inaccurate audio transcription) but weighed them against the efficiency gains (reducing report writing from two hours to 60 seconds) to build a compelling case for a pilot program.
Lesson 2
Anchor innovation in community needs and legislative priorities. Program discovery shouldn't start with a list of available jobs — it should start with where an industry is heading and what the community requires. Research on AI applications for maritime and first responders was intentionally grounded in local mandates: the Seattle 2025–2026 AI plan, Washington state guidelines, and US Coast Guard cybersecurity mandates.
Lesson 3
Value the "discovery" phase, and move in the right direction. Gathering diverse ideas before fully defining a plan is a crucial part of the discovery, development, and delivery process. In periods of uncertainty, having a perfectly solid plan matters less than being prepared to move in the right direction — much like the 1850s directive to just "go west."
Lesson 4
Adopt a "practitioner publisher" model for outreach. To reach prospective students pre-inquiry, the university can empower faculty and students to act as "practitioner publishers." Sharing helpful, free content (how-to tutorials on YouTube, GitHub, etc.) builds trust, demonstrates a caring attitude, and creates an organic funnel that introduces people to the brand before explicitly marketing degree programs.
Lesson 5
Complement, don't disrupt, the core academic business. Experimental initiatives — the proposed AI Lifelong Learning Institute (AILI) or stackable micro-credentials — should serve as a testing ground that complements core degree programs rather than interrupting the current academic business model.
Lesson 6
Recycle old ideas for the modern era. Past successful initiatives shouldn't be discarded. Correspondence education once reached students on submarines or cargo ships; 50-minute "brown bag" lunch classes in 1972 served the community. Old concepts can be repurposed and modernized to serve alumni and community members seeking continuing education.
Lesson 7
Maintain an international perspective. Technology and industries like maritime operate globally, so program development and academic discussions must always include international contexts — e.g., how AI impacts locations such as Korea, Vietnam, Canada, and Mexico.
Throughline: across all seven lessons, the common thread is momentum with guardrails — move forward on real community/legislative need, mitigate rather than avoid risk, and build trust-first before selling.

Important Phrases & Quotes

"Yes, and"

Borrowed from classic improv — encourages teams to acknowledge risks while simultaneously finding ways to mitigate them, rather than letting concerns stall projects.

"Discovery, development, and delivery"

The phased framework used to describe the approach for exploring and launching new initiatives.

"Go west"

An 1850s analogy: during periods of uncertainty, having a perfectly solid plan is less important than simply moving in the right direction.

"Practitioner publisher"

Industry professionals with their own brand or community who publish free content (how-to tutorials, etc.), serving as an organic funnel for the university.

"Pre-inquiry" / "Pre-funnel"

Activities — workshops, micro-lessons, free content — that build academic credibility and reach prospective students before they ever formally inquire about a degree.

"Blue tech" ("wet" vs. "dry")

Terms discovered during the discovery phase describing the maritime technology industry — distinguishing land-based tech ("dry") from tech out on the water ("wet").

"Blue wave"

A pun describing the incoming surge of opportunities and connections the university is experiencing in the maritime space.

"Hidden in plain sight"

Used by a significant maritime figure to describe the maritime industry's presence in Seattle.

"Earn your degree while at sea"

A marketing slogan pitched during the meeting to promote educational opportunities for those working on ships.

"Traffic light framework"

A proposed green / yellow / red categorization system to help the police department safely navigate the risks of piloting AI technologies.

"Forward deployed"

A military-style phrase highlighted from the meeting's chat, describing the university's proactive, on-the-ground approach to engaging with industries and communities.
