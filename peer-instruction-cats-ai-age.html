Peer Instruction & CATs in the AI Age

Eric Mazur's Peer Instruction and Angelo & Cross's Classroom Assessment Techniques were designed to rescue students from passive lecturing in physical classrooms. In an online environment with generative AI, the challenge has fundamentally changed: AI can now mimic the output of learning — a correct multiple-choice answer, a well-phrased summary — without the student doing any cognitive heavy lifting.

🎓 Pedagogy 🤖 AI-Resistant Design 🌐 Online & Async 📋 Faculty Resource
Core thesis: To keep these methodologies powerful online, design must shift from evaluating the final product to capturing the live human process. The goal is no longer to see if a student can produce a correct explanation — it is to reveal how a student navigates their own misconceptions when confronted with automated information.
Section 1

Peer Instruction (Eric Mazur)

Mazur's core workflow relies on the ConcepTest: a conceptual multiple-choice question designed to expose misunderstandings. Students vote individually, debate with a peer, and vote again. The shift in votes — not the final answer — is the evidence of learning.

⚠️ AI Vulnerability in Online Classes

A student at home during a synchronous Zoom session — or working asynchronously — can effortlessly feed a conceptual question into an AI and receive the correct answer alongside a highly convincing rationale. Peer discussion becomes a mechanical exercise of reading an AI response to a classmate.

How It Needs to Change

  • 🐛
    Move from "Find the Answer" to "Debug the AI." Give students a prompt and a flawed AI-generated explanation. The ConcepTest becomes: "AI generated this explanation. Which line contains a fatal conceptual flaw?" This forces students to critique logic rather than retrieve a fact.
  • 🤝
    AI as the "First Peer" (Asynchronous Flipped Design). In an async forum, allow students to use AI to test their initial hypothesis. Have them write down their initial thought, ask an AI tool to argue against them, and document how their thinking evolved from that friction.
  • ⏱️
    Enforce Live "Cameras-On" Rapid Breakouts. If synchronous, use tight 2–3 minute unrecorded breakout rooms of 2–3 students immediately after a live poll. Time pressure must be tight enough that consulting an AI in a separate window introduces more friction than authentic human dialogue.
  • ✍️
    Capture the Shift, Not Just the Vote. Use collaborative tools where students don't just vote A or B, but must type a single sentence explaining why they changed their answer from Vote 1 to Vote 2.
Section 2

Classroom Assessment Techniques (CATs)

Angelo and Cross built CATs as quick, low-stakes, often anonymous checks of student understanding — the Minute Paper, the Muddiest Point, the One-Sentence Summary. They were designed to surface confusion before it compounds.

⚠️ AI Vulnerability in Online Classes

In an online class, asynchronous text boxes are an open invitation for automated completion. An instructor's "One-Sentence Summary" on a discussion board is trivially satisfied by pasting the reading into an LLM — entirely bypassing the synthesis step that was the whole point.

Adapted CATs for the AI Age

Each classic technique is re-mapped to leverage or out-index AI.

Classic CAT AI Age Online Adaptation Why It Works
The Minute Paper What is the most significant thing you learned? The "Aha vs. AI" Voice Note. Students submit a 45-second audio or video snippet via Canvas Studio or Flip, explaining what finally clicked for them. Speaking extemporaneously captures raw, unscripted human cognition far better than polished text.
The Muddiest Point What is still most confusing to you? The Prompt Sandbox. Students paste a confusing concept into an AI tool, ask for an analogy, then submit the analogy plus a critique of where it breaks down. Normalizes confusion while teaching AI literacy; evaluating an analogy requires higher-order thinking.
One-Sentence Summary Synthesize a complex topic into one structured sentence. The Structural Reverse-Engineer. Give students an AI-generated summary. Ask them to identify the core variables (Who, What, Why) and point out what critical nuance the AI oversimplified. Shifts the task from production (which AI excels at) to evaluation and precision editing.
Directed Paraphrasing Explain a concept to a specific lay audience. The Roleplay Feedback Loop. Students instruct an AI: "Act as a 10-year-old. Ask me follow-up questions where my explanation is too complicated." They screenshot the interaction log. Transmutes a passive assignment into an interactive, formative dialogue — the student becomes the true instructor.

Summary: The Paradigm Shift

For both Peer Instruction and CATs to survive online in the age of AI, instructors must stop treating technology as an administrative delivery mechanism — like an electronic scantron — and start treating it as a cognitive partner. Online formative assessment must shift from evaluating whether a student can produce a correct explanation to revealing how a student navigates their own misconceptions when confronted with automated information.
