Segment 2 — Clark's Summary

Where We Landed

Two narratives, ~90 seconds each. The same arc runs through both: we came in with one hypothesis, the evidence changed it, we landed somewhere more credible.

Project 1 — AI for First Responders

Spoken script (~90 sec)

We started where most people start — the efficiency pitch. Vendors are promising that AI can take a two-hour police report down to sixty seconds. We went looking for the evidence to back that up.

What we found instead was a pre-registered, independent randomized controlled trial — published in the Journal of Experimental Criminology — that tested Axon's Draft One on 85 officers across 755 real reports. No statistically significant reduction in writing time. The sixty-second claim doesn't hold up under scrutiny.

That changed everything. If we couldn't lead with efficiency, we had to find the framing that was actually defensible. We landed on accountability and quality assurance — AI as a consistency tool for supervisors, not a narrative shortcut for officers. And we built a governance framework around that: a three-tier traffic light — Green, Yellow, Red — that gives departments clear rules about what AI can do today, what requires a pilot, and what is strictly off-limits regardless of what a vendor promises.

The bottom line: the technology is already in the field. The policy isn't. That gap is the problem — and the opportunity for CityU.

Project 2 — CityU × SPD Partnership

Spoken script (~90 sec)

We went into the SPD meeting expecting to pitch a training program. We left with something more interesting — and more important.

SPD's research team raised the same RCT study we'd found — unprompted. Their own senior research scientist is already skeptical of the vendor efficiency claims. And then we learned that their prior evaluation partner, PNNL, lost its funding last year. They don't have anyone to tell them whether the tools they're buying actually work.

That's the gap CityU can fill. Not as a training vendor — as a research partner, a third-party evaluator, and an education provider. Three roles SPD explicitly said they don't have covered.

We landed on a Year-1 roadmap built around four workstreams: research, evaluation, education, and advocacy. The immediate next step is an on-site visit and a draft MOU. The long-term play is that CityU becomes SPD's independent evaluator as they move from low-risk to medium-risk AI use cases — and potentially extends the same model to Seattle Fire and EMS.

Segment 4 — Live Demo

Open edX Micro-Credential Demo

First CityU STC Summer Program — 13 Korean students. WGU uses the same platform. Demonstrate what a micro-credential looks like in practice before the room imagines something abstract.

What to show (~10 min)

1. The course itself. Open the Impact of AI for First Responders course — walk the room through the structure: 5 modules, 18 lessons, built for complete beginners in emergency services. Show the module sequence so the Roundtable can see the full arc at a glance.

2. Highlight two modules by name. Module 4 (Traffic Light Framework) directly ties back to the research presentation — same framework, now embedded in a learnable course. Module 5 (Compliance) covers the WA State and CJIS requirements — signals that this isn't generic AI literacy, it's jurisdiction-specific.

3. The Summer Program context. Frame it: this is the first CityU STC program, 13 Korean students, already running on Open edX locally. Not a proposal — proof of execution. The course content exists; the platform is tested.

4. The WGU connection. WGU uses Open edX at scale — this isn't an experimental tool. It's the same infrastructure used by one of the most recognized competency-based institutions in the US.

5. The Blue Tech bridge. The natural next question is: what micro-credentials make sense for first responders and maritime workers? The answer is already here. CityU built one. Cloud deployment is the next step — not a blocker to the concept.

Why both plot as Executive Issues

AI for First Responders
  • Low familiarity: No department has a proven governance model; vendor claims are unverified; no standard evaluator exists
  • High impact: Brady material exposure, officer liability, King County Prosecutor blocking AI-produced narratives
  • Can't be delegated: Only 42.9% of organizations have formal AI policies despite 55.7% deploying AI tools [1]
  • Deadline pressure: WA State AI Task Force Recommendation 3 — officer attestation requirements active
Maritime / Port Security
  • Low familiarity: Maritime AI adoption is outpacing cybersecurity measures — shipping companies deploying AI faster than defining cyber accountability [2]
  • High impact: Maritime cyberattacks doubled in 2025 [3]; Port of Seattle hit by Rhysida ransomware (Aug 2024, 90K individuals affected, 71K WA residents) [4]
  • Attack window collapsing: Time from vulnerability to exploit dropped from 63 days (2018) to under 15 minutes today [5]
  • Executive order: New federal EO strengthening cybersecurity of US ports, vessels, and waterfront facilities [6]
Talking point — why this framing matters for the Roundtable

The Uncertainty Matrix from Survive, Rest, and Thrive gives us a clean answer to "why is this on the president's agenda and not a department-level issue." Both the first responders work and the maritime signal plot in the same quadrant: low familiarity, high impact — Executive Issue. That's not a judgment call. It's what the variables show. No department has a proven playbook for AI governance in public safety. The legal exposure is real and active. The technology is moving faster than policy in both sectors. This is not business as usual, and it can't be delegated down.

The same matrix also answers "is maritime a distraction?" — it's not a new track we invented. It's another data point confirming the pattern.

Segment 4 — Clark's Reflections

Reflections on the Process

Honest observations about how the research actually went — what changed your thinking, what surprised you.

  • 1
    The evidence broke our opening pitch — and that was the best thing that happened. We came in ready to argue efficiency. Finding the Adams et al. RCT before the SPD meeting — not after — meant we didn't walk into the room with a claim their own research scientist was going to knock down. The discipline of looking for disconfirming evidence before presenting is something we'd do again from the start.
  • 2
    The policy gap is larger than the technology gap. We expected to spend most of our time understanding the AI tools. We ended up spending more time on procurement timelines, WA State deadlines, union bargaining rights, and King County Prosecutor memos. The technology is moving faster than anyone's governance can keep up with — and that's where the real work is.
  • 3
    The conversation with SPD reframed CityU's role. We learned more in that one meeting than in weeks of desk research. The PNNL gap — SPD losing their evaluation partner with no replacement — wasn't in any document. It came out in conversation. That's a reminder that the most important context rarely survives into published reports.
  • 4
    "Blue Tech" is bigger than we scoped it. We started with police and fire. By the end, the same dynamics — AI tools procured faster than policy, frontline workers without training, no independent evaluation — showed up in maritime and port operations too. The framework travels. That wasn't the plan; it's what the research showed us.
Segment 2 + Closing — Blue Tech: Maritime

The Maritime Blue Shift

This is no longer just a signal — it's a developed strategic opportunity. CityU STC as the premier partner for Seattle's Smart Waterfront, anchored by a live federal compliance mandate.

Roundtable framing — how to introduce this

The first responders work gave us a framework and a track record. The maritime sector is where that framework hits a federal mandate — right now, this month. Every terminal operator in Seattle and Tacoma is legally required to prove role-specific cyber training under USCG 33 CFR 101.650. They don't have a training partner. CityU STC can be that partner, and we already have the curriculum model to prove it.

Suzanne's Ask

Organization Name Recommendation

Suzanne asked for a suggestion — avoid "Center." Come ready with a recommendation and the reasoning behind it.

Recommended Top pick

CityU Blue Tech Collaborative

Signals multi-agency partnership (SPD, maritime, future sectors) rather than a single institution's output. "Collaborative" is honest about the model — CityU as convener and evaluator, not sole actor. "Blue Tech" is already your language and mirrors Maritime Blue's ecosystem framing.

CityU Applied Intelligence Lab

Emphasizes hands-on evaluation and research. "Lab" signals rigor without implying permanent infrastructure. Works well if the primary identity is the evaluator role with SPD and future agencies.

CityU Frontline AI Initiative

Broadest framing — covers police, fire, EMS, maritime, and any other frontline sector. "Initiative" is lightweight and doesn't overcommit on structure. Better if you want flexibility to grow scope before formalizing.

CityU Public Safety AI Institute

Most formal and academically credible. "Institute" carries weight with funders and accreditors. Trade-off: implies more permanent staffing and infrastructure than currently exists — sets a higher expectation to meet.

Talking point if asked to explain the recommendation

"Collaborative" fits because it accurately describes what we're building — CityU at the center of a network of partners, not a standalone unit. The SPD model is already collaborative: technologists, social scientists, SPD's research team, potentially the union and the prosecutor's office. The maritime angle adds another partner. The name should reflect that from the start, so we're not renaming it in two years when it outgrows a single-sector identity.

Segment 6 — Clark's Closing

Closing Remarks

~60 seconds. End on the institutional opportunity, not the research summary.

What I keep coming back to is that CityU is positioned in a way that very few institutions are. We're not a vendor, so we don't have a tool to sell. We're not a pure research university, so we're not waiting three years to publish findings. We're applied — which means we can sit across from SPD, or a port authority, or a fire department, and say: we'll evaluate what you're buying, we'll train your people, and we'll tell you the truth about what works.

That's rare. And the demand for it is only going up — because the technology is moving, the policy isn't, and the people doing the work on the ground are being asked to make decisions they're not equipped to make.

The research got us to the door. The question for this group is: how far do we want to walk through it?
