Applied Intelligence Center · Pilot Plan

Helping SPD Make the Case for BWC AI

A roadmap for convincing each internal audience — and the deliverables that back it up. Goal: 90-day body-worn camera AI pilot that keeps officers on patrol instead of at a desk.

🚔 SPD Engagement Project-Based · Faculty-Led 90-Day Pilot June 2026
The chief has the will — he needs the ammunition. Each internal audience has a different fear driving their resistance. The roadmaps below give him the right argument for each room.
🎖️
Command Staff
Core fear: liability — one bad AI report tanks a prosecution
1
Show the real cost of the status quoCalculate patrol hours lost per officer per week to desk report writing. Frame it as a staffing and public safety problem, not a tech upgrade.
2
Present the vendor landscapeShow which tools peer departments are already using. Removes the "we're the guinea pig" objection.
3
Walk through the risk registryEvery known risk paired with a specific mitigation. They need to see the university has already thought through what they're afraid of.
4
Present the pilot askOne precinct, 90 days, university-audited, automatic rollback trigger. Make it easy to say yes.
Key moment: Show the math first. Hours lost × headcount = a number that frames everything else as solving a real problem.
👮
Officers & Union
Core fear: AI gets them in trouble — signing something false
1
Bring union reps in earlyNot after the plan is set — during it. Being consulted is fundamentally different from being notified. This is the single most important sequencing decision.
2
Establish the iron ruleThe AI drafts. The officer reviews and signs. The officer is always the legal author. No exceptions, no ambiguity in the policy language.
3
Distribute the officer FAQPlain-language Q&A on how the tool works, what happens if the AI is wrong, and what opt-in/opt-out looks like during the pilot.
4
Frame pilot as voluntaryOfficers who participate get time back. Officers who don't aren't penalized. Voluntary adoption in the pilot builds organic credibility for a future rollout.
Key moment: Union rep in the room before the pilot design is finalized — not after. Once they feel ownership, they become advocates.
⚖️
City Attorney
Core fear: Brady exposure and discovery complications
1
Deliver the legal brief firstFaculty-authored memo covering Brady implications, how peer jurisdictions handle AI-drafted report discovery, and existing case law. Give them something to react to.
2
Frame the ask as "help us write the guardrails"Not "please approve this." Involving them in drafting the policy language gives them ownership and surfaces objections early.
3
Show the audit trail designDemonstrate that every AI output is logged, timestamped, and preserved for discovery — the AI version is always distinguishable from the officer's final signed report.
4
Secure written sign-off on pilot scopeNarrow written approval on a 90-day, one-precinct pilot is much easier to get than blanket program approval. Start small.
Key moment: "Help us write the guardrails" framing transforms the city attorney from a gatekeeper into a co-author — dramatically increasing approval odds.
🖥️
IT & Records
Core fear: RMS integration failures and chain-of-custody gaps
1
Involve IT in vendor selectionNot after. Share the vendor landscape with IT first and let them flag RMS compatibility issues before a vendor is chosen. Prevents the "we can't integrate this" surprise.
2
Map the data flowDocument exactly how BWC footage moves to the AI, how drafts are stored, and how the final report lands in the RMS. Every handoff needs a clear owner.
3
Confirm chain-of-custody designAI draft and officer-edited final must be stored separately and linked by case number. Records needs to sign off on the retention and disclosure policy.
4
Run a pre-pilot integration testBefore any officer touches the tool, IT validates that data flows correctly end-to-end in a sandboxed environment. No surprises on Day 1.
Key moment: IT as vendor co-evaluator, not downstream recipient. They'll defend a system they helped select.
The university's role is evaluator, not recommender. This landscape gives SPD command staff and IT a common baseline — removing the "we don't even know what we're buying" objection before it's raised.

Evaluation Criteria

University-led scoring framework for SPD's vendor review
Accuracy & Reliability
  • Published error/hallucination rate
  • Performance on noisy audio (TVs, crowds)
  • Accuracy on minority dialect transcription
  • Human review catch-rate in peer deployments
Legal & Compliance
  • Brady / discovery audit trail design
  • Data retention and deletion policy
  • CJIS compliance certification
  • Indemnification language in contract
Integration & IT
  • RMS compatibility (out of box vs. custom)
  • BWC hardware requirements
  • SSO / Active Directory support
  • Data residency (cloud region, on-prem option)
Cost & Deployment
  • Per-seat or per-incident pricing
  • Pilot pricing vs. full contract terms
  • Training and onboarding timeline
  • Exit clause and data portability

90-Day Pilot Timeline

Pre-Pilot · Weeks 1–4
Setup & Stakeholder Alignment
University faculty conduct vendor demos, IT integration assessment, and legal brief delivery. Union briefing and FAQ distribution. City attorney policy drafting session.
  • Vendor selected and contract scoped
  • RMS integration tested in sandbox
  • Union rep briefed and FAQ approved
  • City attorney sign-off on pilot scope
  • Participating precinct identified, officers briefed
  • Audit logging system confirmed
Month 1 · Days 1–30
Soft Launch — Supervised Use
Voluntary officer participation. Every AI draft reviewed by a designated supervisor before officer sign-off. Faculty conduct weekly check-ins and error logging.
  • 30-day accuracy audit by university faculty
  • Officer feedback survey (anonymous)
  • Error rate reported to command staff
  • Union rep debrief on officer experience
Month 2 · Days 31–60
Expanded Use — Officer-Direct Review
If Month 1 error rate is under 10%, supervisor pre-review is removed. Officers review and sign AI drafts directly. Participation may expand to additional volunteer officers.
  • 60-day accuracy and bias audit
  • Patrol hours recovered — first measurement
  • IT stability and RMS sync review
  • Mid-pilot briefing to command staff
Month 3 · Days 61–90
Evaluation & Decision
University delivers final audit report. Command staff and union receive full findings. Decision: expand, modify, or discontinue — based on data, not assumption.
  • 90-day university audit report delivered
  • Patrol hours recovered — final measurement
  • Risk registry updated with pilot findings
  • Recommendation memo for command staff
  • Full department briefing if expanding

Non-Negotiable Guardrails

✍️
Officer Is Always the Legal Author
Every AI output is a draft only. The officer reads, edits, and signs. The signed report is the officer's sworn statement — not the AI's. This is written into the pilot policy and the vendor contract.
🗂️
Immutable Audit Trail
The original AI draft and the officer's final signed version are both stored and linked by case number. The diff between them is preserved for discovery. No AI output can be deleted before retention period expires.
🚦
Traffic Light Scope Enforcement
AI is Yellow tier only during the pilot — human in the loop on every output. Red tier uses (initial charging documents, use-of-force justifications) are explicitly excluded from the pilot scope in writing.
🔁
Automatic Rollback Trigger
If error rate exceeds 10% in any 30-day window, the pilot pauses automatically. Resumption requires university sign-off on a remediation plan. Command staff are notified within 24 hours of any trigger event.
🔒
CJIS Compliance Required
Any vendor selected must be CJIS Security Policy compliant. Data residency, encryption standards, and access controls are verified by IT before the pilot begins. No consumer-grade AI tools.
