Opening Hook

The problem in one sentence

⏱ 0:00 – 0:30
"Right now, a student who Googles 'learn AI engineering' will find YouTube, Coursera, and MIT OpenCourseWare before they find us. That is an existential problem."
  • ▸
    We are invisible at the moment students are deciding where to learn. Discovery happens on the internet before it happens in a brochure. If we're not on page one of a YouTube search for AI skills, we don't exist to that prospective student.
  • ▸

Threat 1 · ~1 min

Our competition is free — and it's winning

⏱ 0:30 – 1:30
  • 1
    Students are learning from YouTube, GitHub, and community forums before they ever consider a degree program. That's not a complaint — it's data. The question is: do we fight it, or do we put STC content in that exact stream?
  • 2
    Bootcamps and online platforms charge $10,000–$20,000 and promise a job in 6 months. Traditional degrees charge $30,000+ and promise a diploma in 4 years. We are being squeezed from both sides. The only way out is differentiation — and our differentiation has to be visible before a student pays for anything.
  • 3
    The students who will enroll in 2027 are watching YouTube right now. If STC faculty are not in that feed, someone else's faculty are — and those students will never reach our enrollment page.
🔴 Bootcamps grew 15% YoY in 2024
🔴 70%+ of tech learners start on YouTube
⚠️ STC has near-zero organic search presence for "AI engineering"
Anchor: "We are competing against free. We cannot win on price. We have to win on trust — and trust is built before enrollment, not after."

Threat 2 · ~1 min

Our curriculum clock is running 12 months behind the market

⏱ 1:30 – 2:30
  • 1
    The standard academic approval cycle takes 6 to 12 months. AI is releasing transformative tools every 6 to 8 weeks. By the time a course on Large Language Models clears curriculum committee, GPT-5 has shipped, agents are mainstream, and students are asking about something we haven't proposed yet.
  • 2
    This is not a criticism of our process — it's a structural mismatch between academic governance and technology velocity. Every school faces this. The ones that solve it first win the decade.
  • 3
    The Content Engine decouples content creation from curriculum approval. We publish a YouTube video on Model Context Protocol this week. We run a workshop next month. By the time the formal course goes through AAC, we already know it works — 207,000 views and a paying workshop cohort are the proof of concept.
Anchor: "We don't have to choose between speed and rigor. The pipeline gives us speed at the top and rigor at the bottom — exactly where each one belongs."

The Model · ~1 min

The pipeline that turns content into enrollment

⏱ 2:30 – 3:30
  • 1
    YouTube is our top-of-funnel. A free, searchable video solves a real problem for a developer or student. They find us — we don't pay to find them. Example: an 18-second GitHub tip — zero production budget — has 175,000 views. That's 175,000 people who now know STC faculty build things that work.
  • 2
    GitHub is the lab. Every video links to a live, open-source repository. That's where watching becomes doing — and doing is what creates retention. For non-technical audiences: think of GitHub as Google Drive, but for code — and the world's largest developer community. Over 100 million developers use it daily. When STC faculty publish on GitHub, it signals to industry partners that our instructors build real things, not just slide decks. It's institutional credibility you can click on.
  • 3
    The Community Hub is the retention layer — the place where YouTube viewers stay in STC's orbit until they're ready to commit. This can be Teams, Slack, or Open edX forums — whatever IT approves. A 500-person community converting at 10% per semester = 50 seminar or workshop seats per term. Zero marketing spend. Community members recruit each other.
  • 4
    Seminars are the academic taste-test — free or near-free events where a prospective student experiences STC's quality of thinking before making any financial commitment. About 20% convert to a paid workshop. Guest lectures, faculty talks, industry panels, demo days. Run 4 per quarter with 40 attendees each and you generate ~40 qualified workshop leads per quarter — from a single 90-minute event.
  • 5
    Workshops are the conversion event — at $99 to $299 a seat with 20 to 50 participants, they generate direct revenue before a single student enrolls formally. Two workshops per quarter = $16,000 to $120,000 per year in standalone revenue, plus a pipeline of pre-qualified enrollment candidates who already trust us.
  • 6
    Open edX is the destination. Content arrives pre-tested, pre-marketed, with a known audience. Students who enter through the funnel complete at 2 to 3 times the rate of cold-admit students. Higher completion = better outcomes = word-of-mouth referrals = the next cohort recruits itself. The funnel feeds itself.
Anchor: "YouTube → GitHub → Community → Seminar → Workshop → Open edX. Each stage earns the right to ask for the next commitment. The student chooses us — every single step."

Community Instructors · ~1 min

Community-driven instructors are our most valuable recruiting target

⏱ 3:30 – 4:15
  • 1
    The most powerful thing we can do is partner with instructors who already have an audience. A community instructor with 5,000 YouTube subscribers publishing two videos a month is worth more to STC than a $50,000 paid advertising campaign. Their audience trusts them. That trust transfers directly to STC's programs when they publish under our banner.
  • 2
    Community-driven instructors don't just teach — they moderate community platforms, run seminars and workshops, maintain GitHub repos, and build the peer ecosystem that keeps students enrolled and completing. This is a fundamentally different faculty role. A GitHub profile, a YouTube channel, an active community presence, and a seminar track record are credentials as meaningful as a CV for this position.
  • 3
    Every community instructor we bring in multiplies the funnel. Their content compounds. Their community recruits. Their courses retain. Their alumni refer. And none of it stops when the semester ends. You can copy a syllabus overnight. You cannot copy three years of trusted community relationships.
📢 5K subscribers × 2 videos/mo ≈ 120K impressions/yr at $0
✅ Community students complete at 2–3× solo enrollee rate
Anchor: "We are not just building a content strategy. We are building a community — one whose trust is the school's most durable competitive advantage."

Close · ~45 sec

What we need — and what happens if we don't

⏱ 4:15 – 5:00
  • 1
    This model is already running. The YouTube videos are live. The GitHub repos exist. The workshop has happened. We have 207,000 views of proof. We are not asking for permission to experiment. We are asking for institutional recognition of something that is already working.
  • 2
    What we need: a framework that formally recognizes public content production as part of the faculty role — and an IP policy that protects both the faculty member and STC. Without that, this model stays informal, scales to one person, and is one resignation away from disappearing.
  • 3
    If we don't build this, someone else will — and their funnel will be feeding their enrollment, not ours. This is not a risk to manage slowly. The audience we don't capture this year is the cohort we don't enroll in 2027.

The Ask — What Decision Are You Seeking?

Three things we need leadership to act on

The pipeline exists. The proof is in the view counts, the workshop recordings, and the live course catalog. The bottleneck is institutional — not technical.

  • Formal recognition of the Practitioner-Publisher faculty role — define public content production (YouTube, GitHub, workshops) as a legitimate and compensable part of the associate faculty workload.
  • An IP and co-branding agreement template — clarify who owns what when faculty publish under their name but with STC institutional support, so the model can scale safely.
  • A small publishing fund or release time — the minimum viable investment to move from one person's side project to a repeatable school-wide system. This does not require a new hire or a new budget line — it requires redirecting existing resources toward what is already working.
