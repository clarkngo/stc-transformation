Framework 2 The 4 Cs of Survival

The basics of Survive mode — implemented proactively during steady-state and tightened when a market shock hits. For STC, a market shock could be a sudden enrollment drop, a major employer pulling back on hiring, or an accreditation challenge.

$
Cash
  • Enrollment runway: How many terms can STC operate at current cost base if enrollment drops 15%? 25%? Know the number.
  • Revenue diversification: Map tuition, tech fees, grants, and corporate contracts — no single source >60% of operating budget.
  • Receivables discipline: Track outstanding student accounts and financial aid disbursement lag; tighten during enrollment dips.
  • Grant pipeline: Maintain 1–2 active federal/state grant applications at all times (NSF, DoL, WA SBCTC) as buffer capital.
Diagnostic: What is STC's current cash runway at flat enrollment? At −15%?
%
Cost
  • Fixed vs. variable faculty mix: Target a sustainable tenure/adjunct ratio that allows flex without layoffs; review annually.
  • Cost-per-student: Track lab licensing, cloud infrastructure, and software costs per enrolled student — benchmark against peer SoTCs.
  • Resist hiring to solve curriculum gaps: Default to course redesign, adjuncts, or industry partnerships before adding FTE.
  • Unit economics: Know CAC (marketing spend per enrolled student), LTV (total tuition per completion), and gross margin per program.
Diagnostic: Which programs have the highest cost-per-credit-hour? Are they also the highest-margin?
◎
Customers
  • Retention cohort tracking: Monitor term-to-term and year-to-year retention by program; flag cohorts below threshold early.
  • Concentration limits: No single program should exceed 30% of total STC enrollment — concentration = fragility.
  • Employer partner risk: Map top 10 employer hiring partners; build mitigation plans for loss of any top-3 relationship.
  • At-risk student signals: Define and act on early-alert triggers (GPA drops, non-payment, attendance) within first 4 weeks of each term.
Diagnostic: Which programs or student segments show declining retention trends over the last 3 terms?
↔
Communication
  • Faculty alignment: Share the honest narrative of STC's AI-first direction — what it means for curriculum, workload, and job security — before people fill the vacuum with fear.
  • Student value story: Articulate clearly why an STC degree still matters in an AI world; embed it in advising, onboarding, and career services.
  • Employer partner cadence: Quarterly touchpoint with top 10 hiring partners; annual co-design session for curriculum priorities.
  • Crisis script: Pre-draft the communication plan for an accreditation challenge or major enrollment drop — don't write it under pressure.
Diagnostic: If enrollment dropped 20% tomorrow, could you explain why and what you're doing about it — to faculty, students, and leadership — within 48 hours?
Framework 3 The BLAST Framework

The recipe for a Thrive organization — executing strategy with agility and learning during uncertain times. For STC, thriving means graduating AI-fluent technologists faster than the market can discount degrees, and doing it with distributed decisions, not bottlenecked committees.

B
Beliefs
Forward-looking bets STC is placing on the market
  • Bet 1: AI-fluent graduates will command a 20–30% salary premium by 2027 — and employers will pay for that signal.
  • Bet 2: Hybrid human-AI teams become the standard hiring unit; pure coders without AI fluency become less hireable.
  • Bet 3: Employers will increasingly co-design curriculum rather than just consume graduates.
  • Test these beliefs quarterly with employer data, salary surveys, and job posting analysis.
L
Learning Velocity
The rate at which STC acquires and applies new knowledge
  • Curriculum cycle time: Measure months from "employer need identified" to "course updated." Target: under 6 months for module-level changes.
  • Faculty PD cadence: Minimum one hands-on AI tool immersion per term for all STC faculty.
  • Feedback loops: Employer advisory board meets each term with specific curriculum questions, not just updates.
  • Fast experiments: Run at least 2 low-cost AI curriculum pilots per academic year before full rollout.
A
Agility
Good decisions, made quickly, close to the action
  • Clarify decision rights: Program chairs should have authority to add AI modules to existing courses without full curriculum committee review.
  • Fast-track certificates: Create an expedited approval path (<30 days) for short AI credential programs responding to market signals.
  • Resource follow decisions: When a program chair makes a curriculum pivot, budget realignment should happen within the same term — not the next fiscal year.
  • Kill the bottleneck: Identify the one approval step that slows every curriculum change; eliminate or delegate it.
S
Shared Context
Common understanding of what matters and why
  • STC identity narrative: Every faculty member should be able to answer "what makes an STC grad different in an AI world?" in 60 seconds.
  • Strategy on one page: Distill STC's AI-first direction into a single reference artifact that chairs, advisors, and faculty all carry.
  • Performance transparency: Share enrollment, retention, and employer placement data with all faculty — not just leadership.
  • Student-facing clarity: Prospective students should understand STC's AI curriculum differentiation before they apply.
T
Trust
High reliability — doing exactly what you say you will do
  • Commitment culture: Chairs and faculty make explicit, trackable commitments on curriculum updates — not aspirational timelines.
  • Employer reliability: When STC promises a curriculum change to an employer partner, it ships on schedule or the partner is notified immediately.
  • Accreditor trust: Treat accreditors as partners, not adversaries — proactive transparency builds the trust that creates flexibility in difficult cycles.
  • Internal follow-through: Leadership commitments to faculty (on resources, timelines, workload) are honored — or renegotiated openly, not quietly dropped.
