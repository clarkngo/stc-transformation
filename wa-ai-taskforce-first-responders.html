What Is the WA AI Task Force?

The Washington Artificial Intelligence Task Force was established in 2024 by the state legislature with a primary focus on protecting the individual rights of Washington residents. It operates through eight specialized subcommittees — covering public safety, labor, healthcare, education, transparency, and more — and engages subject matter experts, industry representatives, labor unions, and the public.

Its outputs are not suggestions. They are formal recommendations submitted to the legislature and governor for action. The task force is also recommending the creation of a permanent statewide AI oversight body — likely housed in the Attorney General's Office or WaTech — because AI evolves too fast for a temporary task force to keep up.

🎙 From the Recorded Meetings

The task force explicitly adopted the NIST AI Risk Management Framework as its foundational governance model — adding an emphasis on "public purpose and social benefit" to address public sector use. This is the same framework that the Green / Yellow / Red traffic light model in the research brief maps directly to.

Section 02

Recommendation 3: Law Enforcement AI Disclosure

This is the recommendation that most directly affects police officers — and it has already been approved for submission to the legislature. It is not a proposal. It is a decision.

🔒
Status: Approved — Submitted to Legislature

Recommendation 3 was formally voted on and approved by the task force. It is now before the Washington State Legislature for action ahead of the July 1, 2026 deadline.

3
Law Enforcement Agencies Must Disclose AI Use
Public Safety Subcommittee · Approved

Law enforcement agencies across Washington are required to formally disclose which AI systems they are using. The specific disclosure requirements are deliberately scaled to reflect the actual capabilities and resources of each agency — acknowledging that a well-funded department like Seattle PD has vastly different capacity for AI disclosure than a smaller agency in Eastern Washington.

  • 1
    Public AI disclosure — agencies must publish which AI tools they deploy and how they are used
  • 2
    Officer attestation required — if an officer uses an AI tool to assist in writing a report, they must personally review the document and attest that the generated information is entirely accurate and correct before submission
  • 3
    Capability-scaled requirements — small rural agencies are not held to the same disclosure infrastructure standard as large urban departments; requirements fit the agency's actual capacity
  • 4
    Human oversight is non-negotiable — the AI assists; the officer owns the output, legally and professionally
🎙 From the Recorded Meetings

The attestation requirement was debated extensively. The core argument: an officer's signature on a report is a legal document. If the AI invented a fact and the officer signed without reading, that officer bears full professional and legal liability — not the software vendor. The task force treated this not as a technology question but as a professional accountability question.

The scaling provision came from recognizing that a mandate built for Seattle would be impossible for a 12-person department in Okanogan County. The intent is universal human oversight, not uniform bureaucratic burden.

What This Means for SPD
  • ▸
    Every AI-generated report (Axon Draft One, Code Four) requires officer sign-off with attestation
  • ▸
    Public-facing disclosure of which AI tools the department uses
  • ▸
    Officers using AI without understanding it are now legally exposed
What's Still Missing Locally
  • ▸
    SPD, SFD, and EMS have no published attestation language yet
  • ▸
    No approved AI tool list exists — Green / Yellow / Red is still absent
  • ▸
    No training program is in place to prepare officers to review and attest AI output
Section 03

Recommendation 4: First Responders Have a Seat at the Table

Under current law, public sector workers — including police, firefighters, and paramedics — can only bargain over the impacts of AI after it has already been deployed. Recommendation 4 changes that.

🤝
This is a rights question, not just a policy question

Aligned with proposed legislation HB 1622, Recommendation 4 gives public sector workers the right to collectively bargain over the initial decision to adopt AI — before deployment — whenever the technology affects wages, job security, or performance evaluations.

4
Public Sector Collective Bargaining Rights Over AI Adoption
Labor Subcommittee · Approved · Aligned with HB 1622

Current Washington law treats the adoption of new technology largely as a management right. Workers can only negotiate the impacts of AI after it has been deployed. This recommendation closes that gap — requiring agencies to bring workers to the table before any AI tool is adopted if it will affect their wages, job security, or how their performance is evaluated.

  • 1
    Pre-deployment bargaining — not post-deployment. Workers must be consulted during the decision phase, not just cleanup afterward
  • 2
    Scope: wages, job security, performance evaluations — if AI changes how an officer is assessed or what tasks they perform, that triggers bargaining rights
  • 3
    Prevents punitive AI use — AI cannot be deployed to intensify workloads, surveil worker performance, or justify discipline without worker input
🎙 From the Recorded Meetings

Labor representatives argued forcefully that broad governance frameworks consistently center on business users while treating workers as an afterthought. Their specific point: "When AI changes how your performance is measured, that's not a technology question — that's a labor condition."

For first responders specifically, this means: if a department deploys AI that tracks response times, grades report quality, or flags behavioral patterns — officers should have union input before that system goes live. Currently, they don't.

Section 04

Recommendation 7: AI in the Workplace — Transparency & Protection

A three-part package that requires employers to tell workers when AI is being used in decisions that affect them — and gives workers the right to understand and appeal those decisions.

7
AI in the Workplace: Transparency, Safety & Accountability
Labor Subcommittee · Three-Part Package
Part A — Multi-Stakeholder Advisory Group

Creates a group including workers, unions, employers, and state agencies to collaboratively develop guiding principles for AI in the workplace — before mandates are written.

Part B — Safety & Upskilling

AI must enhance worker well-being — not intensify workloads punitively. Any AI adoption must be paired with training, reskilling, and upskilling programs for workers whose jobs are affected.

Part C — Transparency & Appeal Rights
  • ▸
    Workers must be informed when AI is used in decisions that affect their jobs
  • ▸
    Understandable summaries of the data used in AI decisions must be provided so workers can evaluate them
  • ▸
    Appeal rights — workers can challenge AI-driven decisions affecting their employment
  • ▸
    Carefully scoped to protect businesses from disclosing trade secrets — the balance between transparency and proprietary information was explicitly debated
🎙 From the Recorded Meetings

For first responders, Part C is the most immediately relevant. If a department uses AI to flag underperforming officers, grade report quality, or optimize shift assignments — that officer now has a legal right to know the AI was involved, understand what data drove the decision, and challenge it. This didn't exist before this recommendation.

Section 05

What Was Actually Debated in the Meetings

The recorded meetings reveal the tensions behind the final recommendations — who pushed for what, who pushed back, and where the real disagreements were.

🤝 Labor vs. Industry

  • Labor: workers are treated as afterthoughts in most AI governance frameworks
  • Industry: transparency requirements must not force disclosure of trade secrets or proprietary training data
  • Result: both sides won — transparency requirements passed with IP protections carved out

⚕️ Healthcare Parallel (Direct Analog to Public Safety)

  • AI cannot be the sole mechanism to deny, delay, or modify health services
  • Any adverse decision must be reviewed by a licensed professional
  • Direct parallel: same principle applies to AI-generated police reports — a human must own the output

💰 The Fiscal Tension

  • Washington faces current budget deficits
  • Several members worried recommendations requiring new funding would stall
  • Resolution: long-term vision framing + immediate low-cost actions identified in parallel

🏫 Community Colleges Called Out By Name

  • Identified as vital hubs for AI upskilling and reskilling
  • Described as facing an "existential" threat amid budget cuts and bureaucratic complexity
  • Key point: the task force specifically recommended using training programs already developed by WA State Community and Technical Colleges

🤖 CSAM and AI-Generated Content

  • Current laws require an "identifiable" person — AI-generated CSAM exploits this loophole
  • Law enforcement is hindered from prosecution without metadata watermarking
  • Outcome: metadata watermarking discussed as a legislative fix

🌐 Permanent Oversight Body

  • Task force acknowledged AI moves too fast for a temporary body to govern
  • Recommended creating a permanent statewide AI advisory body
  • Proposed home: Attorney General's Office or WaTech
🎓
The STC Opening — Named in the Meetings

The Education & Workforce subcommittee explicitly identified Washington State's Community and Technical Colleges as the recommended delivery mechanism for AI upskilling. The task force noted these institutions "intimately understand the state's school systems, student populations, and strict compliance requirements." This is not a general reference — it is a direct mandate for institutions like STC to lead AI literacy training for the public sector workforce.

Section 06

What Happens Next

The recommendations are approved. The legislative clock is running. Here is where things stand.

✅ 2024 — Task Force Established
Legislature creates the WA AI Task Force

Mandated to develop policy recommendations covering labor, public safety, healthcare, education, transparency, and economic innovation. AG's Office leads.

✅ 2025 — Subcommittees Active
Eight subcommittees hold recorded working sessions

Stakeholder input gathered from labor unions, tech industry, healthcare, law enforcement, and education. Recommendations drafted, debated, and revised.

✅ April 2026 — Recommendations Approved
Rec 3 (Law Enforcement), Rec 4 (Bargaining), Rec 7 (Workplace) voted and approved

Submitted to the legislature and governor for action. The task force also formally recommended creating a permanent AI oversight body.

⚠️ July 1, 2026 — Implementation Deadline
State-level action expected

Agencies — including law enforcement — are expected to begin compliance with Recommendation 3's disclosure and attestation requirements. Departments without training, attestation language, or approved tool lists will be scrambling.

2026–2027 — Permanent Oversight Body
Statewide AI Advisory Body established

Ongoing monitoring of AI impacts, continuous policy refinement, and legislative guidance — designed to keep pace with AI's rate of change rather than playing catch-up.

The Mandate Is Clear — The Training Isn't There Yet

Recommendation 3 requires officer attestation. Recommendation 4 requires worker input before deployment. Recommendation 7 requires transparency and appeal rights. None of these can be met without AI literacy training — and community and technical colleges are specifically named as the delivery mechanism.
